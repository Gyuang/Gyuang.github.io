Abstract 
15 
Highly multiplexed imaging technologies can quantify the expression of 10s-100s of proteins and their 
16 
modifications at sub-cellular resolution. Applications to tissue specimens across a broad array of 
17 
pathologies have enabled patient subtyping in terms of novel cell states and spatial cell communities 
18 
and linked their prevalence and overall architecture to patient outcomes. While highly multiplexed 
19 
imaging analysis workflows typically summarize each cell in terms of its post-segmentation mean 
20 
expression, additional cellular information can be quantified including cell morphology, sub-cellular 
21 
expression patterns, and spatial cellular context, ultimately giving a multi-modal view of each cell. While 
22 
deep latent variable models such as variational autoencoders (VAEs) are well established for other 
23 
multi-modal single-cell assays, their ability to integrate these multiple views of a cell from highly 
24 
multiplexed imaging data remains largely unknown. 
25 
 
26 
Here, we explore the abilities of multi-modal VAEs to learn unified latent cellular representations from 
27 
multiple views of each single-cell quantified from highly multiplexed imaging, including mean 
28 
expression, morphology, sub-cellular protein co-localization, and spatial cellular context, while 
29 
conditioning on technical and batch specific effects. We first discuss strategies for training and 
30 
hyperparameter optimization of such models across a set of highly multiplexed imaging datasets of 
31 
breast and melanoma cancers. We next quantify the relevance of the integrated multi-modal latent 
32 
space in predicting patient-specific clinical outcomes and demonstrate competitive performance across 
33 
a range of outcomes compared to existing baselines. Then, we explore the ability of the cellular 
34 
representations to learn cellular phenotypes that align with known cell types and find that the 
35 
expression-specific latent representation can identify clusters more closely aligned with known 
36 
lymphocyte and epithelial subpopulations compared to established workflows. Finally, we test the ability 
37 
of each representation to perform consistent batch integration across different cohorts and discuss the 
38 
considerations on taking known background variables into account.

Introduction 
45 
Highly multiplexed imaging (HMI) technologies can quantify the expression of 10-100  proteins and their 
46 
modifications 
within 
single 
cells 
while 
retaining 
their 
spatial 
locations. 
These 
include 
47 
immunofluorescence based assays such as cyclic immunofluorescence which use optical imaging1,2, 
48 
and mass cytometry based assays such as Imaging Mass Cytometry (IMC) and Multiplexed Ion Beam 
49 
Imaging3,4. These technologies have revolutionized cancer research by elucidating spatial tumour 
50 
heterogeneity with significant consequences for patient outcomes5,6. The standard analytical workflow 
51 
of HMI data includes cell segmentation that identifies single cells within the image and generates a cell 
52 
segmentation mask, containing cell IDs and their spatial locations. Most studies subsequently cluster 
53 
cells based on the mean protein expression and use spatial information contained in these images to 
54 
detect patterns in tissue architecture2. However, cellular states and functions are additionally 
55 
determined by protein subcellular localization, cellular morphology, and the influence of local spatial 
56 
architecture within a tissue7–9.  
57 
  
58 
Deep learning methods are gaining popularity as analysis tools for HMI data but have been largely 
59 
limited to image segmentation and cell type annotation10. Improved segmentation using deep learning 
60 
in HMI data allows for accurate identification of cells within a tissue image which is crucial for cell type 
61 
assignment and further downstream analysis has revealed distinct communities in the tumour 
62 
microenvironment (TME) associated with clinical outcomes11–13. Deep learning methods have also 
63 
improved and automated identification of major cell types in tissues14–16, but these methods cannot be 
64 
used to identify variations within these major cell types as they are limited to using single-cell protein 
65 
expression alone and do not take into account the spatial context of the cells. Some studies incorporate 
66 
spatial information and the tissue context to understand cell states17,18 but these ignore the impacts of 
67 
protein subcellular localization on cell state and differences in cell morphology. 
68 
  
69 
Spatial transcriptomics (ST) and single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) have seen more application 
70 
of deep learning methods, largely for batch correction and spot deconvolution with the aim to identify 
71 
cell types and states19–21. Further, existing deep learning methods integrate different modalities such as 
72 
totalVI, which integrates cell surface protein expression with transcriptomics data22 and DeepST and 
73 
SpatialDIVA which use data from histology and spatial transcriptomics images to identify spatial 
74 
domains of clinical importance23,24. Many of these models follow a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) 
75 
framework and have shown state-of-the-art performance in integrating multi-modal data inputs, 
76 
enabling the classification of cell states not identifiable by a single data type alone25. VAEs are 
77 
generative deep unsupervised models that learn a latent space—a compressed lower dimensional 
78 
representation of the high dimensional input data—that tries to strike a balance between accurate data 
79 
reconstruction and model generalizability26–28. The lower dimensional latent representation learnt by a 
80 
VAE captures relevant features in the data that can be used for downstream analysis such as clustering 
81 
and cell state identification as well as associations with clinical variables using patient data29,30. 
82 
 
83 
Here, we propose a multi-modal VAE for highly multiplexed imaging data (hmiVAE) that learns 
84 
combined and mode-specific latent representations from input mean expression, morphology, protein 
85 
nuclear co-localization, and spatial cellular context, while conditioning on technical and batch specific 
86 
effects. We apply this model to several IMC datasets of breast and melanoma cancers5,6,31 and quantify 
87 
the extent to which the integrated multi-modal latent space is predictive of specific clinical outcomes 
88 
compared to existing approaches. Next, we show that our expression-specific representation can 
89 
identify cell clusters more closely aligned with known lymphocyte and epithelial subpopulations 
90 
compared to established workflows. Finally, we test the ability of our model to perform consistent batch 
91 
integration across different cohorts. Together, this study provides the first exploration of using multi-
92 
modal VAEs to learn meaningful cell representations from highly multiplexed imaging data and 
93 
demonstrates its utility on several common downstream analysis tasks.

95 
Exploring hyperparameter effects and unveiling cellular heterogeneity using multi-view 
96 
integration with hmiVAE 
97 
We first developed a data processing pipeline to extract four views from IMC data: (i) mean protein 
98 
expression, (ii) protein nuclear co-localization, (iii) cell morphology and (iv) cell spatial context. We 
99 
extract these views for each cell within an image. First, we extract mean protein cellular expression by 
100 
taking the mean of the protein expression across all the pixels belonging to a cell. Second, we use the 
101 
per-pixel protein expression counts and find the correlation between these and the mean nuclear score 
102 
as a proxy for nuclear co-localization. Third, we compute the cell morphology features using image 
103 
processing libraries in Python (Methods). Finally, we summarise the expression counts, nuclear co-
104 
localization, and morphology for the k nearest neighbours of each cell to get the spatial context. 
105 
Furthermore, highly multiplexed imaging data suffers from technical artefacts such as speckles resulting 
106 
from non-specific antibody binding and uneven signal intensities across samples32. To control for such 
107 
batch effects, we condition on sample specific identifiers and likely technical artefacts by including a 
108 
one-hot encoding of cells belonging to each sample and computing covariates such as per-sample 
109 
mean image intensity (Methods). These views and covariates form the input to our highly multiplexed 
110 
imaging variational autoencoder (hmiVAE). hmiVAE builds on a standard VAE architecture consisting 
111 
of two neural networks, an encoder and decoder. The encoder takes in each view (expression, nuclear 
112 
co-localization, morphology and spatial context) and batch-specific information to first learn a separate 
113 
representation for each view and then concatenates them to learn an integrated latent space. The 
114 
decoder then samples from this integrated latent space to reconstruct each view (Fig. 1A & Methods). 
115 
The integrated latent space, as well as the view-specific embeddings can then be used for downstream 
116 
tasks such as cell type interpretation and associations with clinical variables. 
117 
  
118 
As tuning hyperparameters is a key aspect of successfully training any  deep learning method33,we 
119 
considered how varying a set of parameters affected the validation loss. Specifically, we selected (i) the 
120 
number of hidden layers, (ii) the size of the hidden layers (i.e. hidden layer dimension), (iii) latent space 
121 
dimensionality, (iv) batch size, and (v) the weight of the KL divergence term with a warmup and constant 
122 
schedule (Methods) and applied grid search over these to test how each impacted the reconstruction 
123 
likelihood. We found that implementing a schedule on the weight resulted in  better reconstruction than 
124 
keeping it constant throughout training (Fig. 1C), a result also observed in other studies22,34. We also 
125 
noticed that while the dimensionality of the latent space and the number of hidden layers did not have 
126 
a large effect on the reconstruction likelihood (Fig. 1D&E), a larger hidden layer size resulted in better 
127 
reconstruction (Fig. 1F). Similarly, having a smaller batch size during training also resulted in better 
128 
reconstruction (Fig. 1G). We trained the models with the best-performing hyperparameters for each 
129 
dataset, Jackson-BC and Ali-BC which are breast cancer IMC datasets from Jackson et al. (2020)5 and 
130 
Ali et al. (2020)6 respectively with epithelial and stromal marker panel, and Hoch-Melanoma which is a 
131 
melanoma IMC dataset from Hoch et al.(2022)31 with an immune marker panel (Methods, 
132 
Supplementary Table S1). The resulting latent spaces from hmiVAE can be used for downstream 
133 
analysis19,22,23. Using leiden clustering from the single-cell analysis Python library, scanpy35 (Methods), 
134 
we clustered the resulting latent spaces, we refer to these as integrated clusters below.  
135 
  
136

137 
corresponding to each view (Methods) by the magnitude of their association with each integrated 
138 
cluster (Methods). Visualizing the top three features per cluster shows that for each dataset many 
139 
clusters had similar average expression profiles but differed in the localization of different proteins or 
140 
the context of their neighbouring cells (Fig. 2). In the Ali-BC dataset (Fig. 2B), integrated clusters 12 
141 
and 21 both represent stromal cell clusters based on expression of Vimentin and Smooth muscle actin 
142 
(SMA). However, they differ in terms of their spatial context: cluster 12 indicates stromal cells 
143 
surrounded by apoptotic epithelial cells, given by cluster 12’s association with the expression of

Expression-only embeddings improve cell type interpretation 
154 
Cell type annotation represents an important step in IMC data analysis workflow to provide insights into 
155 
the cellular compositions of the tissues. Since this is commonly performed using the mean expression 
156 
of proteins, we aimed to evaluate the efficacy of using the expression-specific cell representations from 
157 
hmiVAE for this task and compared it to cell types found by clustering over the mean protein expression 
158 
values using a standard community detection algorithm, Louvain36 and a popular clustering method, 
159 
FlowSOM37 (Methods). 
160 
 
161 
For the Ali-BC and Jackson-BC datasets that contain markers for major cell type lineages within 
162 
epithelial, stromal and immune compartments, hmiVAE identified distinct clusters corresponding to 
163 
CD3+ T cells, CD20+ B cells and CD68+ macrophages (Fig. 3 A&B). For Ali-BC, FLowSOM and 
164 
Louvain found a general immune cluster which showed a mix of CD45, CD3 and CD68 markers (Fig. 
165 
3A). For Jackson-BC, FlowSOM found two clusters that corresponded to T cells and general immune 
166 
cells, while Louvain found a cluster which corresponded to B cells (Fig. 3B). Majority of clusters found 
167 
by all methods represent luminal epithelial cells. In both datasets, hmiVAE identified a distinct basal 
168 
epithelial cell cluster with clear expression of CK5 and CK14. Louvain clustering found two basal 
169 
epithelial cell clusters in the Ali-BC one of which shows a mix of other stromal markers like SMA while 
170 
the other only shows expression of CK14. In the Jackson-BC dataset, Louvain finds a basal epithelial 
171 
cell cluster only showing expression of CK14 with low expression of CK5 (Fig. 3). For both datasets, 
172 
FlowSOM resulted in many clusters which did not show high expression of any markers and therefore 
173 
could not be assigned to any cell types represented by the marker panels. 
174 
 
175 
To further compare the clusters found by hmiVAE to those found by Louvain and FlowSOM, we treated 
176 
the single-cell type assignments from Jackson et. al (2020)5 and Ali et al. (2020)6 as the ground truth 
177 
labels for the Jackson-BC and Ali-BC datasets. For the Jackson-BC datasets, we also retrieved manual 
178 
single-cell annotations by two independent annotators for a subset of 500 cells from Geuenich et al. 
179 
(2021)14. We contrasted clusters with ground truth using the Adjusted Rand Index (ARI), a metric that 
180 
quantifies the similarity between two clusterings of data points38. We found that for both datasets, 
181 
hmiVAE achieves the highest ARI when compared to Louvain and FlowSOM indicating that it is better 
182 
able to recapitulate the original, published cell type assignments (Fig. 4C). For the Ali-BC dataset, the 
183 
ARI for hmiVAE is comparable to FlowSOM, but this is expected since FlowSOM was used in the 
184 
original publication for clustering (Fig. 4B). In the comparisons with the manual-labelled single cell 
185 
annotations, we found that only by using the expression embedding from hmiVAE to cluster, we were 
186 
able to correctly identify B cells, macrophages and endothelial cells, while Louvain and FlowSOM failed 
187 
to correctly identify these cell types in the subset of 500 cells. Louvain showed higher precision but 
188 
similar recall in calling basal epithelial cells while hmiVAE had higher precision but lower recall for 
189 
stromal cells. All methods had very similar precision and recall for luminal epithelial cell populations. 
190 
This highlights that the expression-only cell representations learnt by hmiVAE may allow for the 
191 
discernment of subtle immune and epithelial cell subtypes.

Clusters from integrated space are associated with clinical variables and patient 
241 
survival 
242 
Existing approaches have used the latent space of a deep unsupervised models to encode spatial 
243 
information and marker expression to derive patterns that provide clinically meaningful differences 
244 
between patient groups18,29,39,40. Therefore, we examined whether the presence of the integrated 
245 
clusters was associated with clinical variables such as stage, grade and cancer subtype 
246 
(Supplementary Table S1) and survival. We first established two metrics quantifying the presence of 
247 
cells from a specific cluster within a patient’s tissue sample:  (i) cluster proportions, which can be 
248 
described as the relative abundance of a cluster compared to others, and (ii) cluster prevalence per 
249 
mm2 of tissue, which can be seen as a measure of a cluster’s density within the tissue (Fig. 5A, 
250 
Methods). We calculated these metrics both the integrated clusters from hmiVAE as well as a baseline 
251 
of the FlowSOM and Louvain clusters when using the full input feature set. We also compared with the 
252 
clusters found by hmiVAE using only the expression feature embedding and the clusters found by 
253 
FlowSOM and Louvain using the expression features only. For both prevalence measures, we 
254

256 
Across the Jackson-BC and Ali-BC datasets, we observed that although the difference between the 
257

258 
integrated clusters exhibited stronger associations with clinical variables compared to clusters from 
259

261

262 
Our findings suggest that the integrated latent space learned by hmiVAE may provide a more robust 
263 
representation of cellular phenotypes associated with clinical variables compared to traditional 
264 
clustering methods. 
265 
To quantify the association between cluster prevalence and patient survival, we evaluated the 
266 
concordance indices of Cox proportional hazards (CoxPH) models applied to clusters obtained as 
267 
described above while controlling for disease stage. We observed that the hmiVAE integrated clusters 
268 
consistently showed the highest concordance indices across all datasets, indicating better predictive 
269 
performance in survival analysis (Fig. 6A). Furthermore, the superior performance of hmiVAE 
270 
integrated clusters was statistically significant compared to directly clustering the entire feature set, 
271 
particularly when using the cluster prevalence per mm2 of tissue. This significance was consistent 
272 
across all datasets, suggesting that the integrated latent space learned by hmiVAE captures meaningful 
273 
biological variation associated with patient outcomes. Specifically, in the Ali-BC dataset, the difference 
274 
in concordance index between the hmiVAE integrated clusters and other methods was statistically 
275 
significant using both measures, further supporting the robustness of our model. 
276 
We next interpreted the biological features of the clusters with significant hazards ratios for both cluster 
277 
prevalence measures in Ali-BC and Hoch-Melanoma (Supplementary Fig. 4&5). In the Ali-BC dataset, 
278 
hmiVAE found a cluster (integrated cluster 0) whose higher prevalence was significantly associated 
279 
with higher risk based on a Cox proportional hazards model for both cluster proportion and tissue 
280 
prevalence. According to marker expression, this cluster consists of luminal epithelial cells expressing 
281 
the hypoxia marker, CAIX, and the proliferation marker, Ki67. Given the depletion of myoepithelial cells 
282 
(expressing SMA, CK5 or CK14) in this cluster and its spatial context (Fig. 1), we hypothesized it may 
283 
represent the hypoxic tumour core41. To investigate this further, we examined images from patients with 
284 
high and low presence of cluster 0 in their tissue sample (Fig. 6 & Supplementary Fig. 10). We found 
285 
that samples with a high presence of cluster 0 had high expression of the tumour cell marker panCK 
286 
and hypoxia marker CAIX, with little to no expression of SMA, CK5 or CK14. Also, in the few regions 
287 
that showed expression of SMA, the luminal epithelial cells were not surrounding the tumour cells. On 
288 
the other hand, for patients with a low number of cluster 0 cells, samples had high expression of SMA, 
289 
CK5 and CK14, low expression of panCK or CAIX, and the luminal epithelial cells were surrounded by 
290 
myoepithelial cells (Fig. 6B). This spatial pattern supports our hypothesis that cells belonging to cluster 
291 
0 found by hmiVAE might belong to the tumour core. 
292 
In the Hoch-Melanoma dataset, integrated cluster 10 was significantly associated with survival for both 
293 
measures whereas the hazard ratio for integrated cluster 15 was only significant using cluster 
294 
proportions. We found that cluster 10 represented tumour cells expressing and surrounded by cells 
295 
expressing IDO, which has been implicated in degrading T cell function42. These tumour cells were also 
296 
surrounded by T regulatory cells (given by the neighbourhood expression of FOXP3) and macrophages 
297 
(denoted by the neighbourhood expression of CD11b), both of which are canonical markers of a 
298 
immunosuppressive microenvironment43. In contrast, cluster 15 represented tumour cells without such 
299 
markers in their neighbourhood. A patient sample that had a high number of cells belonging to both 
300 
clusters exhibited a mixed pattern with cells closer to IDO expressing cells belonging to cluster 10 and 
301 
those away from these cells belonging to cluster 15 (Supplementary Fig. 7). We note that such spatially

304 
Our analysis demonstrates that the integrated clusters derived from the hmiVAE framework exhibit 
305 
significant associations with various clinical variables and patient survival outcomes across multiple 
306 
datasets, highlighting their potential as robust biomarkers for clinical stratification. Further investigations 
307 
with larger datasets and additional statistical tests may provide deeper insights into the significance of 
308 
these findings. Our results demonstrate the value of incorporating multi-view information and leveraging 
309 
deep learning approaches for more accurate and clinically relevant insights into cellular phenotypes 
310 
and their associations with patient outcomes in complex biological systems.

Latent space projection with hmiVAE 
332 
The latent space of a VAE captures the salient features that explain the variation in high dimensional 
333 
data26,44. Therefore, given shared original features in the high dimensional space, we sought to 
334 
investigate how similar and transferable the learnt latent space and feature embeddings were between 
335 
two IMC datasets. To do so we used the Ali-BC and Jackson-BC datasets, which share an antibody 
336 
panel. We trained hmiVAE on one dataset (reference dataset) and used it to generate latent space and 
337 
view-specific cell representations for the other dataset (query dataset). We did this by first clustering 
338 
cells from the reference dataset using their latent space representations or view-specific embeddings 
339 
and assigning them to a cluster ID. These latent cell representations or view-specific embeddings and 
340 
their associated cluster IDs were then used to train a K Nearest Neighbour model (KNN)45, we call this 
341 
the hmiVAE-KNN model. We then used the hmiVAE model trained on the reference dataset to generate 
342 
latent space representations and view-specific embeddings for cells in the query dataset and these 
343 
were then fed into the trained KNN model to generate cluster IDs. This approach was compared to a 
344 
baseline model where we clustered the cells from the reference dataset using their original features—
345 
full (including features from all views), expression, nuclear co-localization, morphology and spatial 
346 
context—and trained the KNN model using the resulting cluster IDs, we call this the baseline-KNN 
347 
model. We then used this baseline-KNN model to generate cluster IDs for cells in the query dataset 
348 
using their original features (Methods). For ground truth comparisons in the case of using the latent 
349 
space learnt by hmiVAE, we compared the labels for the query dataset from the hmiVAE-KNN model 
350 
to the cluster IDs resulting from training hmiVAE on the query dataset and clustering the cells to assign 
351 
cluster IDs. In the baseline-KNN case, we used cluster IDs generated by directly clustering the cells 
352 
from the query dataset using their original features for comparison. We applied KNN using both cosine 
353 
and Euclidean distance metrics and compared clustering results to ground truth (hmiVAE or baseline) 
354 
using the Adjusted rand index (ARI) and a balanced ARI score which adjusts the ARI score to weight 
355 
underrepresented and overrepresented classes similarly46 (Fig. 7A). 
356 
Our analysis revealed distinct performance trends based on the feature sets used. ARI and balanced 
357 
ARI also did not differ much except in the case of morphology-only features for hmiVAE-KNN especially 
358 
when projecting cells from Ali-BC onto the morphology-only embedding learnt from Jackson-BC (Fig. 
359 
7B&C). We notice that the baseline-KNN model consistently achieved higher ARI and balanced ARI 
360 
scores when applied to the full feature set and nuclear co-localization features in both datasets. While 
361 
hmiVAE exhibited superior performance in clustering when leveraging expression and spatial context 
362 
features (Fig. 7). Furthermore, the choice of distance metrics did not influence the projection capability 
363 
of the features learnt by hmiVAE but did when using the original features, especially for the full feature 
364 
set and morphology features. This delineation underscores the importance of considering which 
365 
features are being used for projection. Latent space projection is considered a beneficial potential 
366 
extension for applications in biology47, but our study demonstrates that this might not be so 
367 
straightforward when projecting cells from a different dataset onto a latent space or view-/modality-
368 
specific embedding.

Discussion 
382 
Here, we have investigated the use of VAEs for multi-view modelling of IMC data. Our approach 
383 
revealed integrated clusters corresponding to diverse cellular phenotypes characterised by subtle 
384 
variations in protein expression, subcellular localization, and microenvironmental context. Notably, our 
385 
findings highlight the necessity of incorporating nuclear co-localization, morphological features, and 
386 
spatial information to capture the complexity of cellular states more accurately than from using 
387 
expression alone. We show that the view-specific embeddings and integrated latent representations for 
388 
each cell led to the identification of clusters which had strong associations with clinical variables and 
389 
patient survival, as well as improved performance for common downstream tasks such as cell type 
390 
annotation. We note that while this investigation followed the standard approach of extracting single 
391 
cells and identifying cell-level phenotypes, other studies have improved the performance on  
392 
downstream tasks by creating pixel-level annotations and thereby increasing robustness of the features 
393 
extracted48. This approach could be used to adapt our input features, such as pixel-level protein 
394 
expression and spatial context, and to investigate the effects on the learnt representations and the 
395 
performance on downstream tasks. Further, in this study, our exploration of hyperparameters in the 
396 
hmiVAE architecture unveiled effects on reconstruction performance, emphasising the importance of 
397 
dynamically adjusting the weight of the KL-Divergence term and optimising hidden layer size and batch 
398 
size for superior reconstruction. Leveraging these insights, we identified optimal hyperparameter 
399 
configurations for each dataset, maximising reconstruction likelihood to construct integrated clusters 
400 
representative of the underlying cellular heterogeneity. However, we  considered only a subset of 
401 
possible hyperparameters that could be expanded in future work to contain the learning rate known to 
402 
have a big impact on model performance49. In our case, it could also be beneficial to vary the size of 
403 
the view-specific embeddings given the differences in the number of features for each input view. 
404 
In our comparisons, all three methods, hmiVAE, FlowSOM and Louvain tended to find clusters with 
405 
higher associations with clinical variables when run on the set of features from all four views. Further, 
406 
hmiVAE was able to find distinct spatial patterns with poor survival outcomes without any prior 
407 
knowledge, indicating that this information is contained in IMC data and can be learned with deep 
408 
learning approaches in an integrated way. This shows the importance of incorporating views beyond 
409 
per-cell mean protein expression to learn cell states associated with disease. We expect that spatial 
410 
patterns and novel cell states learned by hmiVAE can be related to transcriptome or genome data where 
411 
there exist match patient samples. Such a study could reveal whether different genomic programs are 
412 
present in patients exhibiting higher or lower prevalence of specific cell states or spatial patterns, 
413 
thereby increasing our understanding of patient heterogeneity in disease. 
414 
Our analysis demonstrated that while hmiVAE performed well on integrating the different views of HMI 
415 
data and a variety of downstream analysis tasks, the applicability of such a method was not without its 
416 
limitations. The latent projection analysis showed that the learnt expression and spatial context 
417 
embedding generalised well over datasets with similar panel designs, but this was not the case for the 
418 
integrated latent space and the nuclear co-localization embedding, underlying the need to investigate 
419 
batch effects during projection for the different views. It may also be beneficial to understand how 
420 
network architecture affects this projection of new cells onto the latent space or view-specific 
421 
embeddings. Further work is also required to determine how well the learnt latent space generalises 
422 
over datasets with different panel designs, tissue types and disease conditions as well as which dataset 
423 
characteristics may impact generalisation. Further, using a KNN model for projection analysis,

Methods 
440 
Datasets used 
441 
The Jackson-BC dataset comprised 358 images obtained from a Tissue Microarray (TMA) of tissue 
442 
sections sourced from breast cancer patients, encompassing 802,591 cells and incorporating 36 
443 
proteins, including two DNA intercalators5. The Ali-BC dataset, derived from the METABRIC study52, 
444 
featured 548 images from a TMA of tissue sections of patients with primary invasive carcinoma. This 
445 
dataset included 536,883 cells and utilised 39 stains, including two DNA intercalators6. Notably, both 
446 
datasets shared an antibody panel designed to target epitopes specific to breast cancer, encompassing 
447 
markers for cell cycle regulation, phosphorylation-based signalling, and distinctive markers for 
448 
epithelial, endothelial, mesenchymal, and immune cell types. The Hoch-Melanoma dataset comprised 
449 
167 images extracted from a TMA of biopsies originating from patients with stage 4 or stage 3 
450 
melanoma. This dataset encompassed 989,404 cells and employed 46 stains, incorporating two DNA 
451 
intercalators. The antibody panel for the Hoch-Melanoma dataset included markers for tumour cells, 
452 
alongside those targeting various immune cell types and their activation states31. Each dataset was 
453 
accompanied by comprehensive clinical and survival information, detailed in table S1. 
454 
Feature derivation 
455 
Each dataset consists of a: (1) 3-dimensional expression array which contains the pixel-level protein 
456 
expression counts, (2) cell segmentation mask array which contains cell ID assignments for each pixel 
457 
and (3) channel-to-protein identification dataframe which associates each z-stack in the expression 
458 
array to a protein measured in the experiment. The mask and expression arrays have the same x and 
459 
y coordinates, and the size of the z-axis in the expression array corresponds to the number of channels 
460 
in the channel file.

Expression features: To create the protein mean expression features, we subset to pixels belonging to 
462 
the same cell ID (as contained in the mask array) in the expression array for each protein. We average 
463 
over the counts over these pixels for each protein to give the average count for expression within that 
464 
individual cell. We do this for each cell creating a N x P matrix where N is the number of cells in the 
465 
dataset and P is the number of proteins measured in the experiment. We denote this matrix Y.  
466 
Protein nuclear localization score features: Due to the resolution of IMC, we restricted protein 
467 
subcellular localization to in-nucleus or not in-nucleus. Therefore, this should be treated as a protein 
468 
nuclear localization score. We generate this score by first creating a “mean nuclear stain” by taking the 
469 
average of the two Iridium DNA stains for each pixel. We subset to pixels belonging to the same cell ID 
470 
and then do a pixel-wise correlation between each protein and this mean nuclear stain. A high 
471 
correlation means that the same pixels within a cell have high expression of a given protein and a high 
472 
mean nuclear stain, suggesting that this protein is expressed in the nucleus. We do this for each cell 
473 
creating a N x P matrix where P is the number of correlations (note here that the number of correlations 
474 
equals the number of proteins measured in the experiment). We call this matrix S. 
475 
Morphology features: We use Python’s regionprops function from the scikit-image library53 to create the 
476 
morphology features: area, perimeter and eccentricity. We also adopt the concavity and asymmetry 
477 
features from DeepCell11. We compute these features for each cell, thereby creating a N x M matrix 
478 
where M is the number of morphology features. In our case, M = 5. We call this matrix M. 
479 
Spatial context features: For the spatial context features, we first compute the 10 nearest neighbours 
480 
based on spatial coordinates for each cell using scanpy35,45. Using these neighbours, we create a N x 
481 
N sparse matrix D s.t. Dij = 1 if cell i is the neighbour of cell j and 0 otherwise. We then scale Y, S, and 
482 
M to be such that each feature in these matrices has a mean 0 and variance 1 and concatenate these 
483 
together. The sparse matrix D is then multiplied with this concatenated matrix, with each component 
484 
normalised by the number of each cell’s neighbours. This gives us a N x L matrix, where L = P + C + 
485 
M. We call each row the spatial context of a cell, and denote this matrix by C.  
486 
Background features and one-hot encoding: We create a sample-wise one-hot encoding for each cell 
487 
i.e., for a given cell, the vector is of length R for R number of samples and has a 1 in position r  if the 
488 
cell belongs to sample r and 0 otherwise. Along with the one-hot encoding, we create several 
489 
background covariates. (1) When background stains such as ArAr80 were available, we computed the 
490 
mean background channel staining per cell and correlated this mean background stain with the mean 
491 
protein expression per cell for all proteins. A high correlation between the background stain and a 
492 
protein’s expression across cells would suggest a non-specific staining for that protein. (2) Since 
493 
staining efficiency can vary between tissue samples32, we also computed the ‘mean sample intensity’ 
494 
which we define as the average pixel intensity over all channels for an image. (3) We also computed 
495 
the average value of signal intensity across all the background channels per sample.  
496 
In the case where information about background stains was not available, we computed the sample 
497 
intensity and the average background stain of each protein.  These background stains were defined as 
498 
the mean over a protein’s expression values for 0-labelled pixels (in the mask file, pixels belonging to a 
499 
cell are denoted by a non-zero integer corresponding to that cell’s ID, whereas pixels that are labelled 
500 
as “0” are treated as background). We denote all background features and one-hot encoding covariates 
501 
by b.

503 
hmiVAE follows a Variational Autoencoder architecture26. It consists of two neural networks: an encoder 
504 
and a decoder. The encoder takes in a data matrix corresponding to each view, along with the additional 
505 
covariates outlined in Background features and one-hot encoding and generates a separate embedding 
506 
.

for each of the four views: protein mean expression, protein subcellular localization, cell morphology 
507 
and spatial context. These view-specific embeddings are then concatenated, passed through an 
508

509 
four views. The decoder takes in the latent representation of each cell and any additional covariates 
510

511

512

and only the expression features set for FlowSOM and Louvain. For hmiVAE we used the cluster IDs 
607 
which resulted from clustering over the integrated latent space and the expression-only cell 
608 
representations. 
609 
To get the proportion of cells belonging to a cluster ID, for each sample ID, we counted the number of 
610 
cells that belonged to each cluster ID and divided that number by the total number of cells within a 
611 
sample. This resulted in a number between 0 and 1 for each cluster ID and these numbers all summed 
612 
up to 1. We call this cluster proportion. 
613 
To get the prevalence of each cluster as an instance per mm2, for each sample ID, we counted the 
614 
number of cells that belonged to each cluster ID and divided it by the dimensions of the image belonging 
615 
to the sample ID. To make computation easier, we multiplied the resulting number with 1e6 (assuming 
616 
an image of size 1000x1000 pixels). We call this cluster instances per mm2 of tissue.  
617 
Association with clinical variables: We carried out an association study between the integrated latent 
618 
space and the clinical variables for the Jackson-BC, Ali-BC and Hoch-Melanoma datasets. This was 
619 
only done for hmiVAE as FlowSOM and Louvain only generate labels for each cell and do not generate 
620 
any embeddings. To understand and compare the association between the resulting clusters, we 
621 
conducted an association analysis between cluster prevalence and clinical variables for all three 
622 
methods (hmiVAE, Louvain, FlowSOM) across all three datasets. 
623 
Association of latent space with clinical variables: For association between the latent space and clinical 
624 
variables, for each sample in the dataset, we first calculated the median cell embedding for each 
625 
dimension of the latent space. Next, we selected patients belonging to the same category for each 
626 
clinical variable and ran a logistic regression with all the median latent space values to find the 
627 
associations of each latent dimension with each category for a given clinical variable. 
628 
During this analysis, we found that the inclusion of some latent dimensions for a few clinical variables 
629 
resulted in a singular matrix during computation, so we excluded them and re-ran a logistic regression 
630 
.

641 
Survival analysis using Cox proportional hazards models 
642 
To find the relationship between patient survival outcome and cluster prevalence, we used the python 
643 
lifelines package56 to fit a Cox proportional hazards model including disease stage as a covariate. We 
644 
carried out this analysis for each cluster ID at a time, for both cluster proportions and cluster instances 
645 
per mm2 of tissue. P values for hazard ratios were adjusted by Benjamini-Hochberg correction.  
646 
Projection on latent space and comparison with baseline 
647 
Only the Jackson-BC and Ali-BC datasets were included for this analysis as these share an antibody 
648 
panel. We did this analysis in two ways: use the latent space learnt from the cells in Jackson-BC to 
649 
generate low-dimensional cellular representations for cells in Ali-BC and vice versa. We will call the 
650 
dataset that the latent space belonged to as the ‘reference dataset’ and the dataset that is projected as 
651 
the ‘query dataset’.  
652 
While carrying out this analysis, we needed to be mindful of the different numbers of background stains 
653 
and samples present in both datasets as this would change the number of covariates expected by the 
654 
model. In the case where the query dataset had more background stains than the reference dataset, 
655 
we selected the stains that matched between the two. While in the case where the reference dataset 
656 
had more background stains than the query dataset, we selected all the stains present in the query 
657 
dataset while setting the value for all ‘missing’ stains to zero. In both scenarios, we used a random one-
658 
hot encoding for samples similar to Gayoso et al. (2021)22. This ensured that the number of total 
659 
covariates input to the model was the same between the reference and query datasets. Details of how 
660 
this analysis was carried out is outlined in the Latent space projection with hmiVAE section of the main 
661 
text.
