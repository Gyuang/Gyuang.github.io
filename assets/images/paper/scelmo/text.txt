Abstract
Different from a recent approach to building the Foundation Models (FMs) for
analyzing single-cell data based on the pre-training and fine-tuning framework,
in this manuscript, we extend the concept from GenePT [1] and propose a novel
approach to leverage the advantages from Large Language Models (LLMs) to for-
malize a foundation model for single-cell data analysis, known as scELMo. We
utilize LLMs like GPT 3.5 as a generator for both the description of metadata
information and the embeddings for such description. Moreover, we also com-
bine the embeddings from LLMs with the raw data under both zero-shot learning
framework and fine-tuning framework to handle different tasks. We proved that
scELMo is capable of cell clustering, batch effect correction, and cell-type anno-
tation without training a new model. Moreover, the fine-tuning framework of
scELMo can help with more challenging tasks including in-silico treatment anal-
ysis or modeling the perturbation information. scELMo has a lighter structure
and lower requirement for resources but impressive performance, which poses a
promising path for developing domain-specific FMs.
Keywords: Single-cell data analysis, Foundation Model, Large Language Model,
In-silico Treatment Analysis, Perturbation Analysis.

1 Introduction
Developing large language models (LLMs) or foundation models (FMs) is becom-
ing more and more important for both engineering and science [2–4]. In the biology
area, these models are widely used to analyze information of DNA sequence [5, 6],
representation of cells and genes [7–10], etc. Therefore, FMs can facilitate various
downstream analysis for biological or medical research. Here we investigate a specific
type of biomedical tabular data, known as single-cell sequencing data [11, 12], for its
intersection with FMs. Single-cell sequencing data can help us describe the biologi-
cal activity and information at the cell level. Generally, the observations of single-cell
data are cells, while the features can be many candidates, including gene expression
information [12], protein expression information [13, 14], methylation information [15],
etc.
Several pre-training-based methods were developed to analyze the single-cell data
based on the large-scale sequencing results from different databases. However, as a
recent work, GenePT [1] questions the contribution of only using the sequencing
information for biological analysis. GenePT believes that we can either utilize the
information from the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) [16] as
prompts and get the embeddings of such prompts from LLMs to describe the gene
(GenePT-w), or transfer the cell to sentence by gene ranking and use the ranking
results as prompt to extract the cell embeddings from LLMs (GenePT-s). Based on
the results from GenePT, incorporating pre-existing knowledge or literature of the
features in single-cell datasets is also meaningful to explore. However, GenePT limits
the ability of LLMs and the information from the NCBI database may not be enough
to summarize the function of genes in a well-structured approach. Moreover, the idea
of using GenePT-s to generate cell embeddings is not very practical. The first rea-
son is the high sparsity of single-cell data, so the genes with zero expression are hard
to rank. GenePT-s is also not capable of handling large-scale datasets because of the
usage and time limitation from OpenAI API [17]. Recently, the access of LLMs includ-
ing GPT 3.5 [18], GPT 4 [19], and LLaMa [20] offers us opportunities to explore the
information of these features with the help of LLMs. These models have been widely
used for knowledge summarizing [21], network searching/model design [22], and other
approaches to enhance scientific research [23, 24].
Therefore, we follow the hypothesis of GenePT and explore the ability of using
LLMs to generate meaningful text descriptions of cell-level or feature-level metadata
as well as embeddings of such descriptions. We assume the embeddings from LLMs
carry biological properties and can be utilized in various downstream applications.
Hence, we introduce scELMo, as a pipeline for analyzing single-cell multi-omic data
based on the text description and embeddings directly from LLMs [25]. Using gene
as one example, we use LLMs to summarize the functional information of the given
gene with a suitable prompt and also use the same LLMs to extract the embeddings
of such description. We then can either incorporate the embeddings directly into the
sequenced data by matrix manipulation or combine the embeddings with other models
with fine-tuning targets for various challenging tasks.

2 Results
Overview of scELMo. The high-level idea of scELMo is to transfer the information
of each cell from the sequencing data space to the LLM embedded space. We can finish
this transformation by incorporating information from feature space (for example,
genes and proteins.) or cell space (for example, cell types or cell states). To transfer the
feature information into LLM embedded space, we can either use scientific information
from a known database like NCBI or use the summary given by LLMs as a prompt,
and then generate the embeddings for the prompt based on LLMs’ embedding layers.
Here we choose GPT 3.5 as the tool to summarize the function of features and to
generate embeddings based on our evaluation in the Results section.
After having embeddings for features or cells, we can use them based on the zero-
shot learning framework for clustering or batch effect correction. Moreover, we can
also combine the embeddings with known models to improve their performance for
various downstream tasks. Figure 1 shows the flowchart of scELMo under two different
frameworks. Details of scELMo under different frameworks are summarized in the
Methods section.
Evaluation of Hallucinations in LLMs. The first step of our work is to define
a suitable LLM for generating text descriptions and embeddings. The suitable LLM
should not suffer from Hallucinations [26], that is, generating fake or incorrect infor-
mation about our given feature information or cell information. Here we consider GPT
2 [27], GPT 3.5, GPT 4, LLaMa-2 (70B), Mistral [28], bioGPT [29], Claude 2 [30],
and Bard (PaLM 2) [31] as candidates to generate text description. Considering the
diversity of tokens as well as the access to embedding layers, we utilized GPT 3.5 to
generate embeddings. We sampled 20 proteins and 20 genes from the known proteins
and genes, and used these models to generate the text description of these features.
We verified the correctness of the outputs by comparing them with known databases
(GeneCards [32] and NCBI). The proportion of correct outputs is shown in Figure 2
(a). Moreover, we also consider the time usage for one query for each LLM, shown in
Figure 2 (b).
Based on our results, GPT 3.5 and GPT 4 gave us the most accurate description.
However, GPT 4 required longer query time and some of the outputs from GPT 4 did
not follow our restriction of format mentioned in the prompt. By considering the trade-
off between query time and the proportion of correct outputs, we selected GPT 3.5
as our tool to generate text descriptions of features. Moreover, we also evaluated the
stability of the embeddings of LLM’s outputs based on the same prompt for the same
gene, as well as the similarity of embeddings from different genes. Our results are sum-
marized in Extended Data Figures 1 (a) and (b). According to Extended Data Figure
1 (a), we found that the correlation between arbitrary embeddings from different LLM
outputs of the same gene is higher than 0.9, which implied that the embeddings from
GPT 3.5 have high stability and they can capture the intrinsic functions for each gene.
Moreover, based on Extended Data Figure 1 (b), we figured out that embeddings
from different genes had lower correlation comparing with the embeddings from the
same gene. Therefore, embeddings from GPT 3.5 can also capture the functional het-
erogeneity of different genes. Furthermore, we loaded the gene functional annotation
information provided by Geneformer [9] and used the known information to predict

For the batch effect correction task, since GenePT has already analyzed the per-
formance of such embeddings for single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data, we
focused on single-cell proteomic data, such as sequencing data based on CITE-seq [14]
and CyTOF [13]. Here we considered two scenarios. We tried to use the embeddings
from scELMo and GenePT to reduce the batch effect for datasets from the same pro-
tocols and different protocols. To evaluate the performance of batch effect correction,
we used metrics from scIB [34] and considered the contribution of our embeddings
for reducing batch effect (known as Sbatch) and for preserving the biological variation
(known as Sbio) separately. Figures 3 (a) and (b) display the results for batch effect
correction from two datasets based on CITE-seq and Figures 3 (c) and (d) display
the results for batch effect correction based on one CITE-seq-based dataset and one
CyTOF-based dataset. From these four figures, we found that using the aa approach
could not help on this task and could make the case become worse by reducing these
two scores simultaneously. However, if we used the wa approach, we could reduce the
batch effect proved by the improvement of these two scores. Moreover, incorporating
the cell-type embeddings with aa mode also could not improve Sbatch and Sbio jointly.
However, combining the cell-type embeddings with cell embeddings under wa mode
can also improve the Sbio score for both cases. Therefore, finding a good base embed-
ding space is important for utilizing cell-state embeddings in a proper approach. As an
extension, we summarized our analysis of multi-omic data integration [40] in Appendix
C.
scELMo for cell-type annotation. Cell-type annotation is a foundational prob-
lem in the single-cell analysis [41]. Here we investigated the ability of scELMo to
address this task under both zero-shot learning framework and fine-tuning frame-
work. For the zero-shot learning framework, we incorporated the gene embeddings
into training datasets and testing datasets to access the cell embeddings and used
a kNN classifier implemented by GenePT for the cell-type annotation in the testing
dataset. However, this approach failed when training datasets were combined from
multiple resources or with large batch effects (shown in the PBMC section of Table
1). Therefore, we also constructed a simple classifier based on neural networks [42]
and contrastive learning [43]. We then trained the model based on gene embeddings
and expression profiles. Such model is also known as an adaptor in Natural Language
Processing (NLP) tasks [25]. We tested the ability of cell embeddings from this model
for annotating cell types.
From Table 1, we found that the zero-shot learning ability of embeddings from
GPT 3.5 is impressive for the cell-type annotation in the hPancreas dataset and the
Aorta dataset. Moreover, representing cells by using the rank of genes and using the
representation as prompts cannot help GPT 2 or GPT 4 to give us the correct answers
for the cell types of these cells. Therefore, zero-shot learning based on gene embed-
dings is capable for datasets with no obvious batch effect for cell-type annotation.
However, for the PBMC dataset, which is constructed based on datasets from dif-
ferent sources, the gap between zero-shot learning results and fine-tuning results is

scELMo for in-silico treatment analysis. Using computational simulation
methods to facilitate the discovery of novel targeted therapies and drugs is an impor-
tant task for computer-aided healthcare [45–47]. In this section, we examined the
ability of scELMo to use the adaptor for cell-type annotation and the gene embeddings
we extracted from GPT 3.5 for modeling human disease and further revealing the
potential therapeutic targets. Inspired by Geneformer [9], we finetuned our adaptor
based on the classification task of cell conditions and used the cell embeddings from
the adaptor for therapeutic target discovery. We ensured that our adaptor reached
its best performance and tested the change of embeddings by removing the differene-
tially expressed genes (DEGs) in the expression levels across different conditions. We
discussed the importance of fine-tuning a model in Appendix D. If we remove a suit-
able candidate for targeted therapies, the cell embeddings under disease will be more
similar to the cell embeddings under the control case. The metric we used to evaluate
similarity is the cosine similarity between the average of cell embeddings under the
diseased case and the average of cell embeddings under the control case. To verify the
targets we discovered, we searched the possible research about the target therapies of
certain diseases by literature review, which is summarized in Supplementary File 2.
The first group of diseases we considered are hypertrophic or dilated cardiomyopa-
thy states [48]. The scRNA-seq dataset we used here is known as the Heart dataset.
We determined the genes whose in-silico deletion in diseased conditions could signif-
icantly shift the cell embeddings from disease conditions to the non-failing (NF) or
control condition. The change of cosine similarity before and after the deletion for
cardiomyopathy states is summarized in Figure 4 (a). Based on this figure, our adap-
tor identified two novel potential therapeutic targets for cells under the DCM case
and four novel targets for cells under the HCM case. The silence of genes identified

3 Discussion
Developing methods to model genetic and cellular functionalities is an important task
in computational biology research. One idea is to propose a biological question and
figure out the specific model to handle this question, while another approach is to
have a foundation model to address all the known and unknown biological questions.
In the single-cell data analysis area, recently many researchers tended to pre-train a
large-scale model and declared a foundation model by showing their comparable per-
formance with different task-specific models in certain downstream tasks. However,
it is hard to find tasks that could only be resolved by such pre-training and fine-
tuning framework [44], which questioned the contribution of consuming large-scale
resources to develop such models. Therefore, inspired by GenePT, we figured out
another approach to generalize a foundation model in the single-cell research area,
that is, utilizing the contribution of LLMs to generate meaningful feature embeddings
and cell embeddings. We could either directly utilize these embeddings for clustering
or reducing batch effect, or combine these embeddings with task-specific models to
improve their performance. These two approaches are summarized as scELMo. Access-
ing the outputs of known LLMs like GPT 3.5 did not require many resources, and our
results did show the superiority of scELMo in answering multiple biological questions.
For scELMo under the zero-shot learning framework, we could utilize cell embed-
dings to perform clustering and batch effect correction. These contributions are based
on the fact that embeddings from the text description of features in single-cell datasets
are good for representing biological concepts or functions. We also discussed the factors
that could affect the performance of generating meaningful cell embeddings, including
the approach to compute the average embeddings, the number of cells in one dataset,
the number of features we need, etc. We also proved that such embeddings could be
used for multi-omic data analysis, which posed the power of using LLMs as tools for
incorporating prior information to enhance task-specific analysis.
Considering the limitation of the zero-shot learning framework, we also proposed a
fine-tuning framework for scELMo. By combining the feature embeddings from GPT
3.5 with a light-structured neural network, we could use the embeddings to annotate
cell types and reach a level similar to those of FMs that require a lot of resources
for pre-training and fine-tuning. Moreover, scELMo can also be used for detecting
novel therapeutic targets by observing the change of embeddings corresponding to the
removal of certain genes. We could also directly incorporate the cell embeddings or
feature embeddings with task-specific models for better performance in modeling the
data with perturbation. In-silico treatment analysis and perturbation analysis are two
challenging and important tasks with cell-level knowledge, which further supported
the potential of scELMo and related work.

gene-specific LLM. In addition, since the idea from scELMo is capable of modeling
arbitrary biomedical data with tabular format, we believe that extending the usage of
scELMo to other tasks or areas is also very promising.

For the cell-type annotation and in-silico treatment analysis tasks, we propose a
light-structured neural network with a contrastive learning [43, 64] design. Here T is
a neural network with ReLU [65] as the activation function. Our intuitions come from
the requirement for a good representation of cells with different labels and conditions.
Therefore, we formalize the loss function of our model as:
Ltotal = Lclassifier + λLcontrastive,
where Lclassifier represents the classification loss of the model output as we use cell-
type labels for model training. Lcontrastive represents the contrastive learning loss we
use to distinguish the representations of cells under different conditions in the latent
space. λ is a hyper-parameter and we set λ = 100 in this manuscript to assign a
larger weight for label-aware clustering. We utilize the embeddings after fine-tuning
as training and testing datasets and use a kNN classifier to annotate the cell types to
evaluate the representation we learn based on scELMo.

our targeted gene g as:

the metrics described and implemented by scIB [34]. We compute all the metrics we

in [0,1] and a higher value means better performance.

means better performance.

cosine similarity between the mean cell embeddings from the control case and the mean
cell embeddings from the diseased case. The definition of the score here is described
in the Methods section.

and the application scenarios and analyzed the relationship between the attributes of

First, we directly plotted the visualization results of three different methods for
the hPancreas-train dataset in Extended Data Figure 5. From this figure, we found
that using neither gene embeddings from GenePT nor GPT 3.5 with aa mode could

embeddings from GPT 3.5 with wa mode could preserve the major cell-type-specific
clustering information. Based on this interesting observation, we further compared the
weights used for these two averaging modes based on KL-divergence (KL-Div) [69],
which could reflect the difference for the distribution of gene expression levels in each
cell and a uniform distribution with P =

for both these two cases. Based on Extended Data Figure 7 (a), we found that for all

two and none of them had cells with zero divergence. Therefore, the distribution of
gene expression levels carried more information compared with the weights based on
uniform distribution. Moreover, we also showed that using wa mode was better for
batch effect correction. Therefore, the wa mode is more suitable to handle tasks under
the zero-shot learning framework.

C Analysis of multi-omic data integration.
In this section, we analyzed the possibility of utilizing gene embeddings from GPT 3.5
to resolve multi-omic data integration task. Here we consider datasets from scRNA-seq
and scATAC-seq without paired information. To reduce the dimensions of the scATAC-
seq dataset, we transfer the feature information of such dataset from the space of
peaks to the space of gene activity scores. The visualization results are summarized in
Extended Data Figures 6 (a) and (b). According to these figures, we can still observe
significant batch effect or the difference of cell embeddings from the cells with same cell
types. Therefore, the function of scELMo for multi-omic data integration under the
zero-shot learning framework is not good. Moreover, based on Extended Data Figure
6 (c), neither wa mode nor aa mode can improve the Sbatch score and the sbio score
significantly. Incorporating the cell-type information into the cell embeddings space
can significantly improve the averaged scores, but for metrics like iLISI to evaluate the
mixture of batch information, such embeddings still had zero score. Therefore, scELMo
is not capable of multi-omic data integration under the zero-shot learning framework.
D The contribution of finetuned model in the
in-silico treatment analysis.
In this section, we demonstrated the necessity of using a finetuned model rather than
zero-shot learning for in-silico treatment analysis. In Extended Data Figure 8 (a), we
display the change of CS for the same group of DEGs under the ascending aortic
aneurysm disease and all genes were not significant for the Ascending only state.
Moreover, the change of CS under PCA was nearly constant by varying different genes
for removal, and the change of CS was very small for cell embeddings based on GenePT
across all three states. Therefore, we concluded that the cell embeddings from either
GenePT or PCA were not capable of modeling this disease without the knowledge of
the intercellular variability due to diseases. Moreover, based on Extended Data Figures
8 (b) and (c), such variability was covered by the noise in the original expression space.
Therefore, we need to learn a model that can distinguish the cells under different
conditions as well as generate representative cell embeddings for the inference of novel
therapeutic targets. Our ideas aligned with the strategy adopted by Geneformer.
Moreover, we compared the classification metrics for the cell-level disease condition
under different gene embeddings for these two datasets and displayed the results in
Extended Data Figure 9. From this figure, we found using gene embeddings contain-
ing information from genes generally had better performance than using embeddings
from random numbers. Moreover, the classification results of scELMo based on gene
embeddings from GPT 3.5 were slightly better than the results based on gene embed-
dings from GenePT. Therefore, using gene embeddings from GenePT and GPT 3.5
all contributed to generating representative latent space for different diseases.
