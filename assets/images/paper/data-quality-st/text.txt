Abstract
11
12
Spatial transcriptomics technologies enable high-throughput quantification of gene ex-
13
pression at specific locations across tissue sections, facilitating insights into the spatial orga-
14
nization of biological processes. However, high costs associated with these technologies have
15
motivated the development of deep learning methods to predict spatial gene expression from
16
inexpensive hematoxylin and eosin-stained histology images. While most efforts have focused
17
on modifying model architectures to boost predictive performance, the influence of training
18
data quality remains largely unexplored. Here, we investigate how variation in molecular and
19
image data quality stemming from differences in imaging (Xenium) versus sequencing (Visium)
20
spatial transcriptomics technologies impact deep learning-based gene expression prediction from
21
histology images. To delineate the aspects of data quality that impact predictive performance,
22
we conducted in silico ablation experiments, which showed that increased sparsity and noise
23
in molecular data degraded predictive performance, while in silico rescue experiments via im-
24
putation provided only limited improvements that failed to generalize beyond the test set.
25
Likewise, reduced image resolution can degrade predictive performance and further impacts
26
model interpretability. Overall, our results underscore how improving data quality offers an
27
orthogonal strategy to tuning model architecture in enhancing predictive modeling using spatial
28
transcriptomics and emphasize the need for careful consideration of technological limitations
29
that directly impact data quality when developing predictive methodologies.
30
Keywords: spatial transcriptomics, gene prediction, digital pathology, benchmark, machine learning, deep
31
learning, data-centric AI

Introduction
33
Spatial transcriptomics (ST) technologies enable the measurement of gene expression with spatial context
34
across tissue sections with varying molecular resolution and throughput. These spatial molecular measure-
35
ments are often accompanied by a hematoxylin and eosin (H&E)-stained image of the tissue section used
36
for the ST assay. Although ST experiments can cost several thousand dollars per sample, H&E-stained
37
images are relatively inexpensive and routine in clinical and research histopathology. By leveraging such
38
matched H&E-stained images and gene expression data, machine learning models can be trained to predict
39
molecular information directly from histology images. This greatly extends the utility of both archived
40
and newly generated histology images for molecular inference, potentially bypassing the need for expensive
41
ST technologies.
42
The quality of molecular data can vary widely across ST technologies, reflecting the unique technical
43
constraints inherent to each technology. Imaging-based technologies (for example, STARmap [1], MER-
44
FISH [2], and 10x Xenium) provide sub-cellular resolution and typically higher sensitivity but are currently
45
limited to smaller gene panels and can suffer from optical signal overlap, off-target probe-binding issues,
46
and cell-segmentation errors [3–5]. In contrast, sequencing-based technologies (for example, Slide-seqV2
47
[6], DBiT-seq [7], and 10x Visium) provide transcriptome-wide coverage but at generally lower spatial reso-
48
lution and can be prone to lateral diffusion between spots, problematic spots, as well as PCR amplification
49
biases and drop-outs that yield sparse, noisy counts [8–11]. In addition to these ST technology-specific
50
limitations impacting the molecular data quality, the quality of histology images can also vary through
51
differences in staining protocols, scanner optics, image compression, and other factors that alter color bal-
52
ance, contrast, and effective pixel size across datasets [12, 13]. Although multiple sources contribute to
53
variation in data quality, their collective impact on the performance and interpretability of downstream
54
predictive models remains largely unexplored.
55
A growing body of work has applied deep learning models, varying in terms of the convolutional,
56
graph, or transformer-based architectures used, to predict gene expression from histology images using
57
ST data for training [14, 15]. At first, these approaches were almost exclusively trained and evaluated
58
on data from sequencing-based technologies such as Visium where predictive performance, as measured
59
by the Pearson correlation coefficient between predicted and observed gene expression, often averaged
60
around 0.3. More recent studies have begun training and evaluating models using data from imaging-
61
based technologies such as Xenium, reporting a higher predictive performance [16, 17]. However, prediction
62
performance remains highly variable across datasets, tissue types, and models, highlighting the need for
63
further investigation into factors that impact prediction performance [18].
64
In light of this, our study assesses the impact of training data quality on the performance of gene
65
expression prediction from histology images using deep learning models. To this end, we utilize two previ-
66
ously published breast cancer datasets from serial tissue sections assayed by a sequencing-based technology
67
(Visium) and an imaging-based technology (Xenium) with paired H&E-stained images of the same tissue
68
section [19]. We find that when using Xenium data as the input training data compared to when using
69
Visium data, the average prediction performance across genes increased. To determine what specific data
70
quality factors in the input training data may contribute to the observed prediction performance differ-
71
ences between technologies, we further perform in silico ablation experiments to reduce data quality by
72
introducing expression sparsity and noise and decreasing image resolution on the better performing Xe-
73
nium data to recapitulate poor performance when trained with the worse performing Visium dataset. We
74
further perform in silico rescue experiments in an attempt to enhance molecular data quality of the Visium
75
dataset using gene expression imputation and demonstrate that such augmentation leads to overfitting and
76
poor generalizability. Overall, our results underscore the critical role of training data quality in spatial

Results
80
Data quality benchmarking on paired Visium and Xenium spatial transcriptomics
81
datasets
82
To investigate how ST training data quality affects gene expression prediction from histology images via
83
deep learning, we leveraged serial section breast cancer datasets generated by Visium CytAssist and Xe-
84
nium (Figure 1A) [19]. Because a single tissue section cannot undergo two distinct ST assays, serial sections
85
allow us to control for biological variability and focus on technical variability across the different ST tech-
86
nologies. Still, slight differences in the region of the tissues profiled as well as image and molecular data
87
resolution persist. Therefore, to better ensure that differences in gene expression prediction performance
88
can be attributed to the training regime rather than to confounding variation between the training datasets,
89
we standardize as many aspects of the input as possible. To this end, we first aligned the Visium histology
90
image to the Xenium histology image using STalign, bringing both datasets into a common coordinate sys-
91
tem (Figure 1B) [20]. This enables the molecular data from either technology to be visualized and utilized
92
with either histology image. We then identified the shared region between the two datasets and rasterized
93
the gene expression to a common spatial resolution (Figure 1C), as demonstrated by Aihara et al. [21].
94
By minimizing such sample-specific differences, this preprocessing enables controlled comparisons across
95
all combinations of molecular and imaging data. Using this setup, we extracted histology image patches
96
that correspond to the rasterized molecular data and trained identical deep learning models composed
97
of a pretrained ResNet50 feature extractor, a four-layer multilayer perceptron and a final linear output
98
layer to predict gene expression at the patch level for the 306 genes common to both ST assays (Figure
99
1D). We evaluated model prediction performance by computing Pearson correlation coefficients (PCC) and
100
normalized root mean square error (rMSE) between true and predicted patch-level gene expression on a
101
held-out test set (15% of patches) and, in some cases, on an independent serial section Xenium replicate
102
dataset (Rep2) to further assess generalizability.
To determine which factors of data quality influence
103
prediction performance and interpretability, we performed a series of data augmentations including in-
104
creasing expression sparsity and noise, applying Gaussian blur to simulate lower-resolution images, as well
105
as gene expression imputation in an attempt to remove sparsity and noise, and then reassessed prediction
106
performance as well as Grad-CAM saliency (Figure 1E).
107
Applying this approach, we directly compared the predictive performance of our deep learning model
108
when trained on each technologies’ associated histology image and corresponding molecular data (Figure 2).
109
For every gene, we computed the PCC between the ground-truth expression values and model predictions
110
on the held-out test set, using five independently trained models to account for training variability. We
111
found that the PCC distribution across genes is notably shifted toward higher values, indicative of better
112
prediction performance, when trained on Xenium data compared to Visium data (mean PCC = 0.715 for
113
Xenium vs. 0.519 for Visium; Figure 2A), representing an increased average prediction performance across
114
genes of approximately 38%. Likewise, in the gene-by-gene scatterplot (Figure 2B), almost all points lie
115
above the diagonal indicating that prediction performance when training on Xenium data was consistently
116
higher than with Visium data for almost all genes. Evaluating the complete distribution of ground-truth
117
and predicted expression values for held-out test set patches reveals that the PCC is not driven by outliers
118
(Supplementary Figure 1). To further ensure this trend holds across metrics, we also examined the range-
119
normalized root mean square error (rMSE) and found that gene expression predictions compared to the

Molecular data quality can impact spatial gene expression prediction performance
131
To disentangle the impact of molecular data quality from image data quality, we next held the image data
132
constant while swapping molecular data between technologies. We leveraged the high-resolution histology
133
image from the Visium dataset and trained two identical models: one on Visium molecular data, the other
134
on Xenium molecular data (Figure 3A–B). Under these conditions, Xenium molecular data outperformed
135
Visium molecular data (mean PCC = 0.605 vs. 0.519; Figure 3A), and most genes exhibited higher PCCs
136
when trained with the Xenium molecular data (Figure 3B). We next repeated this with the whole-slide
137
image (WSI) from the Xenium dataset. Again, models trained using Xenium molecular data outperformed
138
Visium molecular data (mean PCC = 0.715 for Xenium vs. 0.492 for Visium; Figure 3C-D). These trends
139
further held for normalized rMSE (Supplementary Figure 3). This demonstrates that, irrespective of the
140
image data, the molecular data used for training has an effect on gene expression prediction performance.
141
To pinpoint what molecular data quality factors may drive these performance differences, we next
142
performed a series of in silico ablation experiments in which we systematically decrease molecular data
143
quality and evaluate its impact on prediction performance. First, we examined expression sparsity, defined
144
here as the proportion of zero counts across patches for each gene. Consistent with previous research, we
145
note that sparsity is more pronounced in the molecular data from Visium than Xenium (Supplementary
146
Figure 4).
Having confirmed Xenium data’s better performance, we increased sparsity in the patch-
147
resolution Xenium gene expression count matrix to match the sparsity in the Visium gene expression
148
count matrix. To do this, we compared corresponding patches from the Visium and Xenium datasets, and
149
wherever the Visium count matrix exhibited a value of ≤0, we set the corresponding patch in the patch-
150
resolution Xenium count matrix value to 0. We repeated this process for Visium expression thresholds
151
of ≤1, ≤5, ≤10, ≤15, and ≤20. As sparsity increased, mean PCC on the held-out test set steadily
152
declined (Figure 3E). To ensure this effect was not an artifact of the held-out test data and to access
153
model generalization on a new dataset, we repeated the evaluation on an independent Xenium serial
154
section (Rep2). Here too, we observed a similar downward trend in PCC as sparsity increased (Figure 3E).
155
Notably, training with Xenium molecular data with increased sparsity using a ≤1 threshold produced
156
comparable performance to when training with the Visium molecular data. Next, we examined expression
157
noise, defined as random fluctuations in gene counts around their true values. To simulate greater expression
158
noise, we added Poisson distributed noise into the patch-resolution Xenium count matrix across a series
159
of rate parameters (λ). As we increased λ to simulate more expression noise, mean PCC on the held-out
160
test set steadily declined (Figure 3F). We again repeated the evaluation on the independent Xenium serial
161
section (Rep2) to confirm this trend. Overall, these results implicate molecular data sparsity and noise as
162
potential drivers of decreased prediction performance.
163
Finally, given that increased sparsity and noise can degrade prediction performance, we asked
164
whether we could rescue the Visium molecular data via gene imputation.

Image data quality can impact spatial gene expression prediction performance
175
To isolate the effect of image data quality from molecular data quality, we held the molecular input
176
constant and swapped histology images between technologies.
First, using Visium molecular data, we
177
trained models on both the Visium high-resolution image (2000px by 1809px)and the higher resolution
178
Xenium WSI (20511px by 27587px). The resulting PCC distributions were comparable (mean PCC =
179
0.519 with Visium image vs.
0.492 with Xenium image; Figure 4A). Gene-level correlations remained
180
along the diagonal (Figure 4B), and normalized rMSE values were similarly low (Supplementary Figure
181
5A), indicating that image resolution had little effect on prediction performance. Next, using Xenium
182
molecular data, we repeated the experiment and observed a larger performance gap (mean PCC = 0.605
183
with Visium image vs.
0.715 with Xenium image; Figure 4C). Gene-by-gene scatterplots (Figure 4D)
184
and normalized rMSE comparisons (Supplementary Figure 5B) demonstrate consistent trends. As such,
185
training on higher resolution histology images can improve prediction performance depending on the quality
186
of the corresponding molecular data.
187
To pinpoint what imaging data quality factors may drive these performance differences, we simu-
188
lated lower-resolution images by applying Gaussian blur with kernels of size 5, 25, and 125 to the Xenium
189
histology images. Under matched conditions, training and testing on equally blurred images, PCC slowly
190
degraded with decreasing simulated image resolution (Figure 4E). These results suggest that image res-
191
olution has a measurable impact on gene expression prediction performance, with progressively blurred
192
images leading to decreased prediction performance.
193
Finally, beyond the impact of image resolution on gene expression prediction performance, we
194
sought to assess its impact on model interpretability. To this end, we employed Grad-CAM [25], which
195
projects prediction gradients onto convolutional feature maps to generate heatmaps that highlight the
196
image regions most influential for a given output.
This technique is especially useful because it can
197
reveal whether the model focuses on potentially biologically meaningful structures, such as cells or nuclei
198
consistent with what histopathologists may focus on, rather than background artifacts. We applied Grad-
199
CAM to predictions for select well-predicted genes with high PCCs including CD4 (PCC = 0.79), a T-cell
200
marker, and PDGFRA (PCC = 0.76), a fibroblast marker (Figure 4F). On high-resolution images, the
201
resulting heatmaps highlighted cellular and nuclear regions. As we introduced increasing levels of blur
202
to simulate reduced resolution, the heatmaps became diffuse and lost alignment with these structural
203
landmarks. These results demonstrate that lower image resolution may not only decrease gene expression

Discussion
207
In this study, we evaluated how data quality impacts deep learning–based gene expression prediction
208
from histology images. We leveraged molecular data paired with H&E-stained images from two different
209
ST technologies, one imaging-based (Xenium) and one sequencing-based (Visium), from serial sections
210
of the same breast cancer tissue thereby representing ST data with varying data quality features. We
211
trained identical deep learning models to predict patch-level gene expression and observed that training
212
on Xenium data can achieve quantifiably better gene expression prediction performance than training on
213
Visium data, with an average prediction performance across genes increase of approximately 38%. To
214
isolate the impact of molecular data quality, we held the histology image constant and found that Xenium
215
molecular data outperformed Visium molecular data regardless of which image was used. In molecular
216
ablation experiments, adding noise and sparsity to Xenium molecular data steadily degraded prediction
217
performance. Conversely, in molecular rescue experiments, imputing Visium data boosted performance
218
on the held-out test set but failed to generalize to an independent Xenium replicate. Likewise, to isolate
219
the impact of image data quality, we held the molecular data constant and found Visium molecular data
220
yielded similar prediction performance on both the Visium and Xenium images, whereas Xenium molecular
221
data benefited from the higher-resolution Xenium image. Simulating lower-resolution imaging by applying
222
Gaussian blur to the Xenium image reduced predictive performance and obscured interpretability via
223
Grad-CAM feature maps. Overall, these findings underscore the impact of both molecular and imaging
224
data quality in deep learning–based gene expression prediction from histology images. Further, our study
225
presents quantifiable metrics of molecular and imaging data quality in terms of their sparsity, noise, and
226
resolution that can be considered in the curation of training data from ST technologies for developing gene
227
expression prediction methodologies.
228
Despite these insights, our study has several limitations. Although our comparative analysis demon-
229
strated that training on Visium data resulted in lower gene expression prediction performance than training
230
on Xenium data, potentially attributable to greater sparsity, noise, and other data quality issues in Vi-
231
sium, Xenium data also exhibits its own limitations in data quality. For example, previous research has
232
demonstrated that imaging-based technologies like Xenium can suffer from off-target probe hybridization,
233
thereby obscuring the target gene’s expression with the off-target gene’s expression [3]. This may result in
234
an increase in the observed target gene’s expression magnitude. Because previous studies have observed
235
that genes with higher mean expression are generally easier to predict, these off-target artifacts could
236
artificially inflate the prediction performance from training on Xenium molecular data [16]. We therefore
237
used the Off-Target Probe Tracker (OPT) with a pad-length of 10 to identify 16 of our 306 genes with
238
potential protein-coding off-target genes [3]. Removing these genes and retraining our models confirmed
239
that they do not drive our observed trends or affect our conclusions (Supplementary Figure 6). Never-
240
theless, comparing predictions of gene expression using datasets from different ST technologies inherently
241
assumes these different ST technologies are measuring the same underlying genes, which can be obscured
242
by off-target probe binding. Hence, future work is still needed to quantify off-target rates as well as develop
243
robust correction strategies to ensure fair, accurate comparisons across ST technologies.
244
Similarly for image data quality, beyond image resolution, other features in H&E staining and
245
slide preparation can impact the performance of deep learning models. In particular, H&E staining for
246
Xenium is acquired after the in situ gene expression profiling is complete, whereas H&E staining for
247

257

261

263

264
Further, our evaluations have been limited to two ST technologies. We focused on 10x Visium and
265
Xenium because, unlike many other current ST technologies, they offer matched H&E-stained images and
266
spatial molecular measurements on the same tissue section. Moreover, the datasets analyzed are derived
267
from serial sections from the same tissue block, thereby allowing us to control for biological variability
268
and focus on technical variability across the different ST technologies. The incorporation of non-serial-
269

270
understanding of how such variation affect prediction performance but would also introduce confounders
271
that limit the interpretability of any observed performance differences. Still, the pipeline we present can
272
be generalizable and applied to future serial section datasets from diverse ST technologies as they become
273
available.
274
Although we found molecular data quality to impact prediction performance, other considerations
275
may come into play in the curation of training data. For example, optimizing for molecular data quality
276
generally comes at the expense of throughput. Therefore, if full-transcriptome gene expression prediction is
277
prioritized, lower-quality datasets with higher throughput may be preferable for training. Cost constraints
278
may also pose challenges to generating high-quality training data. Future work is needed to systematically
279
investigate whether increasing the quantity of training data can compensate for reductions in data quality,
280
and if so, to characterize the rate and extent of such trade-offs.
281
As with many deep learning applications, interpretability remains a key challenge. Although we can
282
demonstrate quantifiable aspects of image data quality that lead to reduced prediction performance, how
283
or why the models perform worse remains unclear. To this end, we applied Grad-CAM to demonstrate
284
that even modest reductions in image resolution disrupted attention on the cellular and nuclear landmarks
285
(Figure 4F), suggesting that cellular and nuclear landmarks may be important image features in prediction.
286
We therefore hypothesized that predictions of cell-type-specific marker gene expression would direct model
287
attention to corresponding cell-type features, analogous to a pathologist’s assessment. However, we find
288
that even when we switched the predicted gene, attention remained fixed on general cellular and nuclear
289
landmarks in a non-cell-type-specific manner (Supplementary Figure 7). This highlights the persistent
290
challenges in interpreting deep learning interpretability methods. Going forward, the development of more
291
robust explainability techniques, whether advances in Grad-CAM, SHAP [32], Integrated Gradients [33], or
292
brand new approaches will be needed for deep-learning interpretability in this setting, though we anticipate
293
such approaches will need to be paired with sufficiently high-quality images.

Methods
305
Datasets
306
Two Xenium datasets (In Situ Replicate 1 and In Situ Replicate 2) along with an adjacent section of
307
a Visium CytAssist dataset of a single breast cancer FFPE tissue block were obtained from the 10x
308
Datasets website for High-resolution mapping of the breast cancer tumor microenvironment using inte-
309
grated single cell, spatial and in situ analysis of FFPE tissue (https://www.10xgenomics.com/products/
310
xenium-in-situ/preview-dataset-human-breast) [19].
Each dataset is accompanied by a histology
311
image. For the Visium CytAssist dataset, both a high-resolution image (2000 pixels by 1809 pixels) and
312
low-resolution image (600 pixels by 543 pixels) are available, with the high-resolution version used in this
313
analysis. The In Situ Replicate 1 Xenium dataset includes a histology image of 20511 by 27587 pixels,
314
while the In Situ Replicate 2 Xenium dataset includes a histology image of 18728 by 27788 pixels. These
315
images are herein referred to as whole-slide images (WSI).
316
The Visium CytAssist dataset included spatial coordinates of the spots already aligned to its his-
317
tology image, whereas the two Xenium datasets required aligning segmented cells to their corresponding
318
histology images.
319
Restricting to shared tissue regions and shared genes
320
We used STalign (v1.0.1) to bring the Visium and Xenium data to a common coordinate system by
321
combining affine transformations with diffeomorphic metric mapping [20]. First, we aligned the Visium
322
histology image (source) to the Xenium image (target) using an affine-only registration guided by eight
323
manually selected landmarks. We then applied the learned affine transform to reposition each Visium spot
324
onto the Xenium histology image. This procedure produced co-registered images of the same dimensions,
325
allowing us to overlay Visium spot-level and Xenium single-cell data interchangeably on either histology
326
image. Next, we registered the Xenium transcript coordinates onto their provided histology image using
327
STalign’s full pipeline: rasterize the mapped transcripts at 30µm resolution, an initial affine alignment
328
based on four manually placed landmarks followed by diffeomorphic metric mapping (parameters: a =
329
2500; epV = 1; niter = 2000; sigmaA = 0.11; sigmaB = 0.10; sigmaM = 0.15; sigmaP = 50; muA
330
= [1,1,1]; muB = [0,0,0]; all other settings default). Finally, we identified the overlapping tissue region
331
between the two technologies (Figure 1B).
332
Likewise, we restricted our analyses to the 306 genes common to both assays, since Visium provides
333
full-transcriptome coverage while Xenium targets a limited panel.

Rasterization preprocessing to obtain common patch-resolution training data
335
After successfully aligning all datasets, histology patches with associated gene expression data were created
336
for input into the deep learning model. For the Visium dataset, each histology patch corresponded to
337
exactly one Visium spot and its associated gene expression values. After aligning the Visium image with the
338
Xenium image, each Visium spot corresponded to a patch size of ∼250×250 pixels. The center coordinates
339
of these Visium patches were then used to generate corresponding patches on the Xenium image. Since
340
the images were aligned, this approach ensured consistency in patch size and location across both datasets.
341
Unlike Visium molecular data, Xenium molecular data corresponds to single cells, meaning each patch
342
contained multiple cells. Therefore, the patch coordinates were used to aggregate (ie. pseudobulk) the
343
Xenium gene expression data into a single spot, analogous to the Visium spots and as done by SEraster [21].
344
The resulting patch-resolution gene expression counts were then natural log-transformed with a pseudo-
345
count of 1, and patches containing zero gene expression were excluded from both the Visium and Xenium
346
datasets. The resulting histology patches, along with their associated patch-resolution gene expression
347
matrices, were subsequently used as input for the deep learning model. There were a total of 3,958 patches
348
for both datasets.
349
Gene imputation, sparsity, and noise data augmentation
350
Numerous gene imputation methods exist, each representing different functional classes [34].
For this
351
analysis, we selected three methods: KNN [22], MAGIC [23], and SCVI [24].
352
KNN-smoothing smooths gene expression by identifying nearest neighbors based on partially smoothed
353
and variance-stabilized expression profiles and aggregating their transcript counts. For this analysis, the
354
number of neighbors was set to k=50, and d=20 top principal components were used to determine the
355
nearest neighbors during each smoothing step. Patch 1587 within the dataset was excluded before applying
356
KNN-smoothing and reintroduced afterward due to insufficient gene counts. The raw UMI counts were
357
used as input, as recommended by the authors.
358
MAGIC (Markov Affinity-based Graph Imputation of Cells) smooths gene expression by employing
359
a graph-based approach, where nodes represent individual cells and edges encode similarities between them,
360
and diffusing gene expression information across this graph. For this analysis, the number of neighbors
361
was set to knn = 50, and the diffusion operator’s power, t, was set to ”auto.” All other parameters were
362
kept at their default values. Log-transformed UMI counts with a pseudo-count of 1 were used as input, as
363
recommended by the authors.
364
ScVI (Single-cell Variational Inference) leverages single-cell RNA-seq data to impute spatial tran-
365
scriptomic gene expression using a neural network. The neural network was trained using the Chromium
366
5’ Gene Expression data from another serial section of the breast cancer dataset, along with 80% of the
367
spatial data. All parameters were maintained at their default values, and the model was trained for a
368
maximum of 300 epochs. Genes TKT, SLC39A4, and GABARAPL2 were excluded because they were
369
absent from the Chromium 5’ training dataset. Log-transformed UMI counts with a pseudo-count of 1
370
were used as input.
371
To introduce sparsity into the Xenium molecular data, Visium patches and their corresponding
372
molecular data are used to impose sparsity on the aligned Xenium patches. Specifically, if a Visium patch
373
contains counts <= t for gene x, the corresponding Xenium patch is set to zero for that gene.
This
374
procedure is applied across all patches and repeated at varying count thresholds t (0, 5, 10, 15, 20). This
375
approach aims to closely replicate the sparsity observed in Visium molecular data within the Xenium
376
dataset.

Neural network architecture and parameters
382
For image feature extraction, we used a pre-trained ResNet50 model with the classification layer removed.
383
The input consisted of extracted patches from histology images, sized 250x250x3 which were resized to
384
the standard ResNet50 input size of 224x224x3. The patches were normalized per channel using the mean
385
and standard deviation values from the pre-trained ResNet50 dataset.
To enhance model robustness,
386
the patches were subjected to random horizontal, vertical, and 90-degree rotations. The output of the
387
ResNet50 model was the last layer of the model, a feature vector of size 2048, representing extracted
388
image features. Once image features were extracted for each patch, we passed them through a four-layer
389
multilayer perceptron (MLP) to both reduce dimensionality and learn the features most predictive of gene
390
expression. Each MLP layer consisted of a linear transformation, batch normalization, ReLU activation,
391
and 20% dropout. The final output is predicted gene expression for all 306 genes per patch. For training,
392
mean-squared error (MSE) loss was used alongside the Adam optimizer [35], with a learning rate of 0.001
393
and weight decay of 0.00001. During training, a batch size of 64 was used, and each model was trained
394
for 150 epochs. The datasets were divided into training (75%), validation (10%), and test (15%) sets. All
395
results shown are on the test sets except the plotted predicted gene expression (Figure 2C) which is on the
396
full dataset. Training was performed on an NVIDIA RTX 6000 Ada Generation GPU.
397
Deep learning interpretability method
398
To interpret the regions of the input image that the deep learning model attends to when predicting spatial
399
gene expression, we employed Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM) [25]. Grad-CAM
400
produces a coarse localization heatmap that highlights the most influential areas of the input image for a
401
given gene prediction. This is achieved by computing the gradient of the target gene score with respect
402
to the feature maps of the final convolutional layer within the ResNet50 architecture. These gradients are
403
then globally averaged to obtain importance weights for each feature map. The weighted feature maps
404
are subsequently combined through a linear summation, and a ReLU activation function is applied to
405
retain only positively contributing regions. The resulting single-channel heatmap is upsampled to match
406
the original input image dimensions, yielding a visual representation of the regions the network considers
407
most relevant for the given gene prediction.
