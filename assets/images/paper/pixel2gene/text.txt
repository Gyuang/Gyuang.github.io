Abstract 
25 
Advances in spatial transcriptomics (ST) have fundamentally transformed our understanding of 
26 
tissue biology by enabling gene expression profiling within intact spatial contexts and uncovering 
27 
tissue organization and microenvironmental interactions. However, current high-resolution ST 
28 
platforms remain constrained by high costs, limited tissue coverage, and technical artifacts, often 
29 
yielding noisy, sparse, and incomplete data that compromise analytical accuracy, biological 
30 
interpretation, and clinical utility. To address these challenges, we introduce Pixel2Gene, a deep 
31 
learning framework that integrates co-registered histology images with ST data to enable 
32 
histology-guided reconstruction and prediction of spatial gene expression. Pixel2Gene enhances 
33 
existing expression measurements by denoising low-confidence data and reconstructing coherent 
34 
expression patterns, while also predicting gene expression in unmeasured tissue regions and 
35 
new samples lacking direct transcriptomic profiling. We systematically evaluated Pixel2Gene 
36 
across multiple high-resolution ST platforms, including Visium HD, Xenium, and CosMx, spanning 
37 
diverse tissue types and disease contexts using downsampling simulations and cross-platform 
38 
comparisons in clinical samples. Across all settings, Pixel2Gene consistently improved data 
39 
consistency, mitigated dropout effects, restored biologically meaningful spatial structure, and 
40 
enabled accurate downstream analyses. By leveraging the scalability and ubiquity of routine 
41 
histology, Pixel2Gene facilitates comprehensive, cost-effective ST profiling at whole-tissue scale, 
42 
supporting large cohort studies, translational research, and next-generation biomarker discovery.

Introduction 
44 
Spatial transcriptomics (ST) enables gene expression profiling within intact tissues, offering a 
45 
powerful framework to investigate cellular organization, molecular heterogeneity, and cell-cell 
46 
interactions in situ1,2. Unlike dissociative single-cell sequencing methods, ST preserves tissue 
47 
architecture, providing critical insights into development, disease progression, and immune 
48 
dynamics3. The field has rapidly evolved from early spot-based platforms with multicellular 
49 
resolution to high-resolution technologies such as NanoString’s CosMx4, Vizgen’s MERSCOPE5, 
50 
and 10x Genomics’ Visium HD6 and Xenium7, which achieve near-cellular or even subcellular 
51 
resolution. These next-generation platforms enable fine-grained single-cell mapping, detection of 
52 
rare cell populations, and characterization of spatial gradients across diverse tissue types, 
53 
substantially expanding the analytical scope of spatial omics. 
54 
 
55 
Despite these advances, high-resolution ST platforms still face significant limitations. High costs, 
56 
lengthy processing times, and restricted fields of view (FOVs) limit scalability and prevent 
57 
comprehensive tissue analysis8,9. Even within profiled regions, incomplete capture of molecules 
58 
results in sparsity and technical artifacts, such as dropout and signal leakage. For instance, 
59 
although Visium HD enables gene expression profiling at 2 µm, 8 µm, and 16 µm bin sizes, the 
60 
resulting expression maps are often patchy and discontinuous, which complicates downstream 
61 
analyses that require coherent spatial patterns. Similarly, CosMx generates sparse and 
62 
fragmented tissue coverage across discrete FOVs, which limits comprehensive tissue 
63 
characterization. These challenges hinder the robust analysis of heterogeneous or large 
64 
specimens, where complete and reliable gene expression maps are most crucial.  
65 
 
66 
Nevertheless, high-resolution ST data do retain substantial biological signals, where many genes 
67 
show consistent and biologically meaningful expression patterns in certain regions, even when 
68 
others are degraded or sparsely captured. This observation presents an opportunity for

71

73 
Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining provides a powerful complement to ST. Routinely used in 
74 
clinical practice, H&E captures nuclear morphology, cellular organization, and tissue architecture, 
75 
representing features that reflect underlying gene expression states. Whole-slide imaging 
76 
achieves subcellular resolution (<0.5 µm/pixel)10 across centimeter-scale tissue sections, with 
77 
rapid turnaround (<24 hours)11 and minimal cost (~$10 per slide)12, making it both high-quality 
78 
and highly scalable. Prior studies have demonstrated strong correlations between histological 
79 
features and transcriptomic profiles, suggesting that histomorphology can serve as a surrogate 
80 
for molecular state13-22. These observations raise an opportunity: by systematically learning the 
81 
relationship between histological features and reliable gene expression in high-quality ST regions, 
82 
one can enhance gene expression quality in low-quality regions and computationally predict gene 
83 
expression where direct measurements are missing or unreliable. Integrating histology with ST 
84 
thus leverages the complementary strengths of both modalities and enables the reconstruction 
85 
and prediction of accurate, high-resolution molecular tissue maps that overcome the limitations 
86 
of current ST platforms. 
87 
 
88 
In this paper, we present Pixel2Gene, a deep learning framework that enhances single-cell 
89 
resolution ST data by integrating co-registered histology images. Unlike prior methods23,24 that 
90 
aim solely to infer gene expression de novo from histology, Pixel2Gene jointly leverages high-
91 
confidence ST measurements and histological features to both enhance existing expression data 
92 
and predict gene expression in regions with low-quality or missing measurements. By integrating 
93 
preprocessed high-quality transcriptomic data with histological features in a unified framework, 
94 
Pixel2Gene reconstructs biologically faithful expression maps that scale to whole-slide tissue

Results 
101 
Overview of Pixel2Gene 
102 
An overview of the Pixel2Gene workflow is shown in Fig. 1. The framework aims to reconstruct 
103 
high-resolution spatial gene expression maps by integrating co-registered H&E images with 
104 
filtered single-cell resolution ST data, to enhance existing measurements and infer expression in 
105 
unmeasured tissue regions. The pipeline begins by aligning the two modalities within a shared 
106 
coordinate system. Here, 16 ´ 16-pixel H&E superpixels, approximately 8 µm ´ 8 µm in size, are 
107 
paired with gene expression data assigned to the corresponding superpixels, either directly from 
108 
8 µm ST bins in Visium HD or by aggregating molecule counts according to transcript coordinates. 
109 
A tissue mask is then derived from HistoSweep25, a high-resolution H&E image quality control 
110 
and preprocessing method to identify biologically relevant regions and exclude artifacts such as 
111 
tissue folds or staining debris, ensuring that analysis focuses on informative tissue areas.  
112 
 
113 
Next, Pixel2Gene extracts multiscale histological features for each superpixel, capturing nuclear 
114 
morphology, local cellular neighborhoods, and broader microenvironmental context. These 
115 
features are used to segment the tissue into histologically coherent regions, which guide quality 
116 
filtering and localized model training. Within each cluster, superpixels with UMI counts below a 
117 
predefined threshold are excluded to retain only high-quality data for robust model learning.

Using these filtered data pairs, a feed-forward neural network is trained to capture the relationship 
120 
between histology and gene expression. The resulting model serves two complementary 
121 
functions: (1) it enhances existing ST by denoising low-confidence measurements, and (2) it infers 
122 
gene expression de novo in unmeasured regions, including gaps within the ST field of view or 
123 
areas lacking transcriptomic profiles. This integrated framework generates enhanced, biologically 
124 
faithful expression maps that preserve native tissue architecture and scale to whole-slide 
125 
specimens, enabling practical analysis of datasets containing hundreds of thousands to millions 
126 
of spatial units (Supplementary Tables 1 and 2). These reconstructed maps enable downstream 
127 
applications such as cell type annotation, spatial domain detection, and discovery of disease-
128 
associated tissue patterns at single-cell resolution. 
129 
 
130 
Downsampling evaluation on human colorectal cancer and mouse brain samples 
131 
To rigorously assess Pixel2Gene’s ability to recover accurate gene expression from degraded ST 
132 
data, we performed downsampling simulations on two high-resolution datasets: a human 
133 
colorectal cancer sample (CRC-P2) from patient 2 and a fresh-frozen mouse brain sample, both 
134 
profiled using 10x Genomics Visium HD. These datasets provided dense, spatially resolved gene 
135 
expression profiles paired with co-registered H&E images, providing an ideal testbed for 
136 
simulating realistic data sparsity while maintaining biological relevance. 
137 
 
138 
Since ground truth expression values are not directly available due to the inherent noise and 
139 
incompleteness of single-cell resolution ST data, we restricted our evaluation to the top 300 highly 
140 
expressed genes, which are less susceptible to dropout and provide a more stable reference for 
141 
assessing reconstruction accuracy. To benchmark Pixel2Gene’s performance, we simulated 
142 
degraded input data by applying a controlled downsampling procedure that mimics spatial 
143 
variability and technical sparsity observed in low-quality ST experiments (Methods).

147

149

153 
 
154 
To quantitatively evaluate Pixel2Gene's reconstruction accuracy, we compared the predicted 
155 
expression values with the original expression values using five complementary metrics: Pearson 
156 
correlation, Spearman correlation, cosine similarity, root mean squared error (RMSE), and mean 
157 
absolute error (MAE). Across all metrics, Pixel2Gene consistently outperformed the 
158 
downsampled inputs, demonstrating robust recovery of biological signal from degraded data (Fig. 
159 
2b). Spatial autocorrelation analysis via Moran's I at multiple scales (16 µm, 32 µm and 64 µm) 
160 
further showed that Pixel2Gene not only recovered, but in some cases enhanced, the spatial 
161 
organization relative to both the downsampled and the original data. These results validate the 
162 
model's dual capability to: (1) denoise technical artifacts, and (2) reinforce genuine biological 
163 
patterns through histology-guided reconstruction.

Pixel2Gene reconstructs complete expression maps from data with sparse coverage 
264 
To further demonstrate the broad applicability of Pixel2Gene, we further applied it to CosMx, a 
265 
high-resolution ST platform that provides subcellular resolution across multiple small, discrete 
266 
targeted fields of view (FOVs). While CosMx achieves high spatial granularity, it is fundamentally 
267 
constrained by its limited spatial coverage. A typical CosMx experiment profiles approximately 
268 
200 discrete FOVs (each 0.5 mm ´ 0.5 mm), covering only less than 5% of a standard tissue 
269 
section (~15 mm ´ 20 mm per slide). Expanding coverage beyond this limit is costly, time-
270 
consuming, and prone to RNA degradation and technical dropout, often resulting in drastically 
271 
reduced molecule capture, with average molecule counts often dropping below 100 transcripts

398 
including data sparsity, technical noise, and incomplete gene and spatial coverage. By integrating

A major strength of Pixel2Gene is its flexible, superpixel-based hierarchical framework. Unlike 
408 
approaches that depend on explicit cell or nucleus segmentation, Pixel2Gene operates directly 
409 
on H&E images, using superpixels with multiscale visual cues to offer robustness to staining 
410

411 
ST platforms, including Visium HD, Xenium, and CosMx, and supports various tissue types and 
412 
gene panel sizes without platform-specific tuning. To obtain cell-level gene expression estimates, 
413 
one can calculate the weighted sum of gene expression for superpixels that overlap with a cell, 
414 
an approach employed by iStar31.  
415 
 
416

417 
data, remain limited in scope and flexibility. GHIST requires nucleus segmentation as a 
418

419

420 
extraction, which may restrict its ability to capture multiscale tissue context. Pixel2Gene avoids 
421 
these limitations by operating on a segmentation-free, superpixel-based representation, and by 
422 
leveraging hierarchical feature extraction to model tissue organization across multiple spatial 
423 
scales. Finally, GHIST has been demonstrated only on Xenium data, whereas Pixel2Gene 
424 
generalizes robustly across multiple platforms and tissue types without reliance on cell-level

Data preprocessing 
663 
Xenium and Xenium 5K. After aligning the transcriptomic profiling data to the post-Xenium H&E 
664 
pixel space, transcripts are aggregated into square spatial bins based on their transformed 
665 
coordinates, with each bin corresponding to a 16 ´ 16-pixel region in the rescaled H&E image, 
666 
referred to as a superpixel, to enable one-to-one alignment with the superpixel grid used for further 
667 
analysis. 
668 
Rescaling. The H&E image is rescaled so that each pixel represents 0.5 µm ´ 0.5 µm. This 
669 
standardization ensures that each 16 ´ 16-pixel superpixel tile corresponds to an 8 µm ´ 8 µm 
670 
area, approximately the size of a single cell. To support hierarchical feature extraction in later 
671 
stages, the rescaled H&E image is padded so that its height and width are both divisible by 256. 
672 
The spatial coordinates of each gene expression bin or cell, expressed in the H&E pixel coordinate 
673 
system, are rescaled using the same factor to maintain alignment between the image and the 
674 
transcriptomic data. Due to technical variation, some Visium HD bins may fall partially outside the 
675 
grid or overlap with others after rescaling; these bins are excluded during preprocessing to ensure 
676 
consistent and reliable spatial mapping. 
677

682 
(ViT) to extract features across scales, beginning with small local patches to capture detailed

688 
Selecting high-quality spatial profiling data 
689 
To ensure only high-quality spatial profiling data are used in the training step, we apply a two-
690 
step filtering procedure that integrates both tissue morphology and gene expression quality: 
691 
 
692 
1. Tissue-detection masking. In this step, we aim to exclude unwanted regions such as 
693 
background, debris, and low-content areas while retaining only histologically meaningful tissue. 
694 
Specifically, we identify and remove (i) background and acellular regions lacking sufficient stain 
695 
or structural content, and (ii) visually irregular, potentially damaged, or noisy regions that may still 
696 
contain some stain but are unlikely to yield reliable data. This is accomplished by analyzing both 
697 
stain density and texture features across the tissue, resulting in a binary mask that preserves 
698 
dense, textured, and well-stained tissue regions. 
699 
 
700 
2. Spatial profiling-quality masking. Beyond histological relevance, we also prioritize regions with 
701 
robust gene expression capture. To achieve this, we first perform K-means clustering on 
702 
superpixel-level histological features to account for tissue heterogeneity. Within each cluster, we 
703 
filter out superpixels whose gene expression profiles, as represented by total UMI counts, fall 
704 
below a specified quantile threshold (e.g., bottom 30%), thereby removing regions likely to contain 
705 
low-quality or noisy molecular data. 
706 
 
707 
By combining these two masks, we ensure that downstream analysis is performed only on regions 
708 
that are both histologically valid and molecularly informative, thereby improving the reliability and 
709 
interpretability of spatial transcriptomic results.

Neural network architecture and hyperparameter tuning 
742 
We employed a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) architecture consisting of a feed-forward neural 
743 
network with four hidden layers, each containing 256 neurons. A leaky ReLU activation function 
744 
with a negative slope of α = 0.1 was applied to each hidden layer to allow for non-linearity while 
745 
avoiding neuron inactivity. The model was trained using the Adam optimizer with a batch size of 
746 
128 for 1,200 epochs. The learning rate was adjusted based on the size of the input training 
747 
dataset to ensure stable convergence across samples of varying coverage.

785 
To evaluate spatial autocorrelation at multiple resolutions, we evaluate spatial autocorrelation at 
786 
multiple resolutions using Moran’s I, a statistic that measures how spatially clustered or dispersed 
787 
the expression signal is, with a fast convolution-based method that handles missing values. To 
788 
simulate different spatial scales, we applied sum pooling to the expression matrix using window 
789 
sizes corresponding to 1´ (no pooling), 2´, and 4´ pooling factors. These correspond to 
790 
neighborhood radii of 16 pixels, 32 pixels, and 64 pixels, respectively, and are reported as Moran’s 
791 
I-16, Moran’s I-32, and Moran’s I-64. At each scale, the pooled expression matrix is passed to the 
792 
Moran’s I function to measure the degree of local spatial autocorrelation. This multi-scale 
793 
approach captures both fine-grained and broader spatial continuity, offering a robust view of how 
794 
well the reconstructed data preserves biologically relevant spatial structure.

Spatial clustering analysis 
797 
We perform spatial clustering in a two-stage manner to ensure both stability and interpretability 
798 
of the resulting clusters. First, we apply MiniBatch K-means clustering to the superpixel-level data, 
799 
intentionally specifying a slightly higher number of clusters than the desired number. This over-
800 
partitioning ensures that fine-grained spatial structures are captured early in the process. 
801 
 
802 
Next, we refine the clustering results using an iterative hierarchical clustering-based merging 
803 
strategy. Specifically, we construct a hierarchical tree based on the centroids of the initial K-
804 
means clusters using a linkage matrix to merge clusters based on a dynamically adjusted distance 
805 
threshold. We then further merge small clusters whose superpixel counts fall below a predefined 
806 
fraction of the total counts (e.g., 0.5%). Samples from these small clusters are reassigned to their 
807 
nearest larger clusters based on Euclidean distances in feature space.
