Abstract
7
Large language model (LLM)–derived gene embeddings, generated from brief NCBI gene
8
descriptions, have shown strong performance in recent biological applications, yet the bi-
9
ological information they contain remains unclear. We evaluate these embeddings using
10
a Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA)–based framework that treats each embedding di-
11
mension as a potential carrier of pathway-level information. OpenAI’s embeddings recover
12
over 93% of Hallmark and C2 pathways, with pathway signals distributed across many di-
13
mensions. Even embeddings generated from gene symbols alone recover more than 64% of
14
pathways, indicating substantial prior biological knowledge embedded in the model. Com-
15
paring 11 small language models reveals that domain-specific models perform best with
16
minimal input, but all models approach OpenAI-level coverage when given modest textual
17
context. Collectively, these results show that LLM-derived embeddings encode unexpect-
18
edly extensive pathway-level information, supporting their use as lightweight, informative
19
representations for downstream biological analysis.

Introduction
21
Transformer-based large language models (LLMs), originally developed for natural lan-
22
guage processing, are now being repurposed to analyze biological data, offering new av-
23
enues for understanding complex biological systems. In particular, their integration into
24
gene-expression data analysis has garnered substantial interest due to their remarkable pre-
25
dictive capabilities across diverse applications in fundamental biological problems (see, e.g.,
26
[1–7] for a review).
27
Current methods for integrating LLMs into gene-expression analysis can be broadly cate-
28
gorized into three main approaches. The first approach leverages the question-answering
29
capabilities of chatbot models like ChatGPT. For instance, Hou and Ji [8] utilized ChatGPT
30
to identify cell types by providing the top ten differentially expressed genes for each cell
31
type and directly asking the chatbot to classify the cell. While this strategy is straightforward
32
and effective for certain applications, it suffers from key limitations. Specifically, ChatGPT’s
33
difficulty in processing and understanding numerical data, coupled with the challenge of
34
designing effective prompts for complex tasks, restricts its applicability to more complex
35
problems, particularly those involving numerical data.
36
The second approach involves training transformer-based foundation models specifically
37
for gene-expression data. Examples include scBERT [9], Geneformer [10], scGPT [11], and
38
scFoundation [12]. These models are trained on extensive collections of single-cell RNA-seq
39
data, typically encompassing thousands of datasets and millions of cells. Once trained, they
40
can be fine-tuned on smaller, user-specific datasets to suit particular applications. While this
41
method offers substantial predictive power in certain applications, its resource-intensive
42
pre-training is inaccessible to most research groups [13], and its performance after fine-
43
tuning often depends sensitively on how well the biological conditions represented in the
44
pre-training data align with those of the target application—an issue that has contributed
45
to the mixed performance reported in recent evaluations [14].
46
The third approach, pioneered by GenePT [15], circumvents the need for training foun-
47
dation models from scratch by utilizing general-purpose LLMs such as ChatGPT. Specifi-
48
cally, GenePT employs OpenAI’s text-embedding function [16] to transform standard NCBI
49
gene descriptions [17] into long, dense numeric vectors known as embeddings. These em-
50
beddings are presumed to encapsulate the semantic content of the gene descriptions (e.g.,
51
functional roles, associated processes, or molecular characteristics), and they can be di-
52
rectly used in downstream tasks. For example, the GenePT method demonstrated effective
53
cell type labeling using these embeddings, and a subsequent method, scHOTT [18], further

optimized this process. More importantly, these embeddings have been successfully applied
55
to solve more complex biological problems, such as predicting the outcomes of gene pertur-
56
bation experiments, yielding substantial gains in modeling efficiency [19–21]. Related work
57
has further incorporated GenePT-style representations—either directly or as a source of in-
58
spiration—into broader multimodal frameworks that integrate gene-expression data with
59
spatial transcriptomics or histopathological image features, supporting applications such
60
as spatial domain identification, cell-state characterization, and disease-related prediction
61
[22–25]. Notably, the same embedding paradigm has also been generalized beyond genes
62
to other biomedical entities, such as clinical concepts in electronic health records, where
63
LLM-derived embeddings of concept descriptions have been used for disease risk prediction
64
[26, 27].
65
Among these three approaches, the embedding-based strategy offers a particularly practical
66
and versatile complement to the others. The text-embedding service of ChatGPT is highly
67
cost-efficient—substantially cheaper than the question-answering API—and recent studies
68
have shown that it can often be replaced by free, open-source LLMs without a notable loss
69
in performance [28]. Moreover, embeddings provide a flexible numerical representation of
70
genes, making them compatible with a wide spectrum of machine-learning techniques, from
71
traditional methods such as logistic regression and random forests to modern deep-learning
72
architectures. These features make the embedding-based approach broadly accessible and
73
easy to integrate into diverse analytical pipelines, while remaining complementary to the
74
strengths of the other two approaches, which excel in different problem settings.
75
However, the advantages of this third approach rest on a critical assumption: that the em-
76
beddings, as numeric vectors, encapsulate rich information about the genes they represent.
77
While the success of various applications using these embeddings suggests the validity of this
78
assumption, it has never been directly validated. In this work, we rigorously investigate this
79
assumption by exploring the extent to which these embeddings capture biologically mean-
80
ingful information about genes. In particular, we assess this by examining whether genes
81
belonging to the same biological pathways exhibit coherent structure within the embedding
82
space.

Methods
84
NCBI Text Descriptions Do Not Automatically Imply Information-Rich
85
Embeddings
86
This subsection illustrates why short NCBI descriptions cannot be assumed to yield information-
87
rich embeddings and therefore motivates the analyses presented in the subsequent sections.
88
Figure 1 (A) and (B) present the NCBI descriptions of two well-studied human genes, TP53
89
and BRCA1. TP53, often referred to as the “guardian of the genome,” encodes a tumor
90
suppressor protein that plays a critical role in regulating the cell cycle, DNA repair, and
91
apoptosis. Mutations in TP53 are associated with a broad spectrum of cancers. Similarly,
92
BRCA1 encodes another tumor suppressor protein, primarily involved in DNA repair. Muta-
93
tions in BRCA1 are strongly linked to an elevated risk of breast, ovarian, and other cancers,
94
making it a key target for genetic testing and targeted therapies. Both TP53 and BRCA1
95
are foundational to cancer research and clinical care, having been the focus of extensive
96
investigations that have revealed a wealth of knowledge about their regulation, interactions
97
with other genes, and roles in cancer biology. However, the NCBI descriptions of TP53 and
98
BRCA1 are relatively brief. Each gene is described in a single paragraph that, while touch-
99
ing upon multiple aspects of the gene’s function, lacks depth and detail. These descriptions
100
provide a high-level overview but do not comprehensively capture the genes’ regulatory net-
101
works, interactions, or pathway involvement—key aspects crucial for understanding their
102
biological roles. Importantly, they are not curated to represent regulatory or pathway-level
103
knowledge, making them an imperfect source for downstream inference.
104
This lack of detail is not unique to TP53 and BRCA1 but is, in fact, a common characteristic
105
of gene descriptions in NCBI. Typically, the NCBI description of a gene is limited to one para-
106
graph, containing no more than a few hundred words. For less-characterized genes, these
107
descriptions are even more concise. As an illustration, Figure 1 (C) shows the description of
108
the human gene A1BG, which appears first in the alphabetical list of human protein-coding
109
genes [29]. The description of A1BG is very brief, consisting of only two sentences.
110
Given the brevity of these descriptions, it is not immediately evident or intuitive that LLM-
111
generated gene embeddings derived from NCBI descriptions can capture comprehensive
112
or even adequate information about genes, particularly their regulatory relationships and
113
pathway associations. This inherent limitation raises critical questions about the extent to
114
which such embeddings can encapsulate biologically meaningful information, warranting a
115
rigorous evaluation.

Pathway Analysis on OpenAI Text-Embeddings of Human Genes
117
We obtained the human gene embeddings generated by the GenePT study from its Zenodo
118
repository. These embeddings were produced using OpenAI’s text-embedding-3-small
119
model [30]. In generating each embedding, the input to the model was the official gene
120
symbol concatenated with its NCBI description (examples shown in Figure 1), and the out-
121
put was a 1,536-dimensional embedding vector, as shown in the top row of Table 1.
122
The primary objective of this study is to determine whether gene-regulatory pathway infor-
123
mation is encoded within the high-dimensional gene embeddings. Our approach is straight-
124
forward: for each of the 1,536 dimensions of the embeddings, we apply Gene Set Enrich-
125
ment Analysis (GSEA) using pathways from the Human Molecular Signatures Database
126
(MSigDB, [31, 32]). For each dimension, genes were ranked by their embedding coordinate
127
values, and GSEA was applied to this ranked list. If a pathway is significantly enriched in
128
a given dimension, we consider this as evidence that the dimension captures information
129
about that pathway. We describe this as the pathway being “reflected” or “represented”
130
by the dimension (not implying that the embedding dimension uniquely encodes this path-
131
way). We further define “pathway coverage” as the proportion of pathways that are reflected
132
by at least one embedding dimension:
133
pathway coverage = number of pathways reflected by at least one dimension
total number of pathways
× 100%
Pathway coverage serves as our primary evaluation metric. A higher pathway coverage sug-
134
gests that the embeddings capture a broader range of pathway-level biological information.
135
This per-dimension analysis allows us to identify which aspects of the embedding space
136
align with known biological processes.
137
For GSEA, we will focus on two widely used collections of gene sets from the Human
138
MSigDB: the 50 hallmark gene sets and the 3,917 canonical pathways in “C2” (curated
139
gene sets). The hallmark gene sets [33] are coherently-expressed signatures derived by
140
integrating many MSigDB gene sets to represent the most well-defined biological states or
141
processes. In contrast, the 3,917 canonical pathways in C2 are curated from various sources,
142
including online pathway databases and the biomedical literature. These pathways encom-
143
pass six main categories: 292 BioCarta, 658 KEGG Medicus, 196 PID, 1,736 Reactome, 830
144
WikiPathways, and 186 KEGG Legacy gene sets. An additional 19 pathways classified as
145
“miscellaneous” are excluded from our analysis.
146
Further, we retain only the protein-coding genes [29] within each gene set and remove
147
any gene sets containing fewer than 10 such genes, as sets with fewer than 10 genes are

GSEA is performed using the R package Fast Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (FGSEA) [34],
153
which allows for false discovery rate (FDR) estimation and control at a user-specified thresh-
154
old. The analysis is conducted independently for each of the 1,536 embedding dimensions,
155
and pathway coverage is then computed by aggregating the statistically significant path-
156
ways across all dimensions. To control the overall false positive rate, we adopt a stringent
157
multiple-testing correction strategy inspired by the Bonferroni method: for each embed-
158
ding dimension, we set the FDR cutoff to p/d, where p = 0.05 and d is the dimension of the
159
embedding. This conservative strategy is intended to minimize false discoveries and avoid
160
overestimating the extent of pathway-level information captured by the embeddings. We
161
choose this highly conservative threshold to avoid inflating pathway coverage due to false
162
positives.

165

166

167

168

169

Assessing the Impact of Using Less Informative Text to Generate Em-
215
beddings
216
The near-perfect pathway coverage suggests that the OpenAI embeddings derived from
217
NCBI gene descriptions encode a substantial amount of pathway-level information. This
218
result is remarkable, given that NCBI gene descriptions are typically concise and contain
219
limited explicit information about gene–gene regulatory relationships.
220
Why might such extensive pathway recovery occur? One possibility is that OpenAI’s text
221
embedding model possesses exceptionally strong capabilities—potentially beyond what hu-
222
mans can extract from such brief descriptions. Such understanding could arise from the
223
model’s ability to compare and contrast descriptions across many genes.
224
Another possibility is that the embeddings are not generated solely from the input text (i.e.,
225
the NCBI gene description), but rather represent a synthesis of the input and the model’s
226
prior knowledge acquired during pre-training. As a transformer-based model trained on
227
massive, diverse language corpora—including extensive biomedical literature—OpenAI’s
228
embedding model may have been exposed to much richer and more explicit biological con-
229
tent than what is available in the NCBI descriptions. This background knowledge could be
230
implicitly incorporated into the embeddings, even when the input text is limited.
231
While the first hypothesis is difficult to test directly and is left for future investigation, we
232
designed an experiment to probe the second hypothesis. Specifically, we generated new em-
233
beddings for each gene using only the official gene symbol as input to the OpenAI embedding
234
tool. We then performed the same GSEA analysis on these “symbol-only” embeddings and
235
compared the results to those obtained using the full NCBI descriptions (hereafter referred
236
to as “full-description” embeddings). To complete the comparison, we also evaluated an
237
intermediate case: embeddings generated using the gene symbol concatenated with only
238
the first half of the NCBI description (defined as the first 50% of words in the description),
239
omitting the second half. We refer to these as “half-description” embeddings. In Figure
240
3(A), we illustrate the workflow used to generate the full-description, half-description, and
241
symbol-only embeddings. Figure 3(B) provides a concrete example using the gene TP53,
242
showing the exact text input used to produce each of the three embedding types.

Assessing the Impact of Using Smaller LLMs to Generate Embeddings
254
To further investigate why OpenAI embeddings derived from NCBI gene descriptions encode
255
such rich pathway-level information—despite the brevity of the input—we examined other
256
LLM-based text embedding tools. Comparing these models may help shed light on the source
257
of the information captured in the embeddings.
258
A recent study by Gan et al. [28] explored the use of embeddings generated by small lan-
259
guage models (SLMs) for predictive tasks in gene-expression analysis. Specifically, the study
260
evaluated ten open-source SLMs—listed from the second to the eleventh row in Table 1 (i.e.,
261
all rows except the first and the last)—each containing only 23 to 55 million parameters.
262
For context, OpenAI’s GPT-3 has 175 billion parameters—over a thousand times larger—and
263
GPT-4 reportedly contains 1.76 trillion parameters.
264
All ten models in the study are general-purpose, with one partial exception: MedEmbed-
265
small-v0.1. This model is based on the general-purpose BGE-small model but was fine-
266
tuned using contrastive learning on synthetic medical question–answer triplets derived from
267
PubMed Central notes. While this fine-tuning improves its performance in clinical and
268
biomedical tasks, MedEmbed-small-v0.1 remains usable as a general-purpose model and
269
demonstrates strong performance on the Massive Text Embedding Benchmark (MTEB) [39].
270
Gan et al. [28] found that embeddings generated by these SLMs from NCBI gene descrip-
271
tions performed comparably to OpenAI embeddings in various predictive tasks, including
272
the prediction of long-range transcription factors, dosage-sensitive genes, and bivalent chro-
273
matin states. This naturally raises a question: Do these smaller models also capture similar
274
amounts of gene-pathway information?

Conclusions and Discussion
316
OpenAI’s gene embeddings, generated from NCBI gene descriptions, have demonstrated
317
exceptional predictive performance in various biological applications. However, it remains
318
unclear how much biologically meaningful information—particularly pathway-level knowl-
319
edge—is actually encoded in these embeddings. Moreover, if such rich information is indeed
320
present, it raises the intriguing question of how this is possible, given the brevity and limited
321
detail of the NCBI gene descriptions. In this study, we provide an initial exploration of these
322
questions.
323
Using a GSEA-based framework, we found that OpenAI’s embeddings achieve pathway cov-
324
erage exceeding 93% for both Hallmark and C2 pathways. Considering the broad and
325
heterogeneous nature of the C2 pathways, this level of coverage is striking. It indicates
326
that a vast majority of pathway-level information is captured by these embeddings, sug-
327
gesting that they are well suited for downstream applications involving gene–gene interac-
328
tions and pathway inference. Furthermore, we observed that even the symbol-only embed-
329
dings—generated using just the gene symbol—capture over 64% of the Hallmark and C2
330
pathways. This result points to the substantial prior biological knowledge retained in the
331
OpenAI embedding model, likely acquired during pretraining.
332
To better understand why OpenAI’s embeddings capture such a vast amount of pathway-
333
level information—despite being derived from relatively short gene descriptions—we ex-
334
amined embeddings generated by small language models (SLMs), which differ markedly
335
from OpenAI’s model in both scale and pretraining data. While the Results section outlined
336
four key empirical findings from this comparison, here we reflect on what these findings
337
reveal about the underlying factors contributing to pathway-level knowledge in different
338
embedding models.
339
First, the strong performance of the symbol-only embeddings from BioBERT-base-cased-v1.1
340
is expected. This model is the only one in our study that was pretrained exclusively on a

356

357

358

359

361

363

364

365
short texts. Their ability to discern nuanced relationships and semantic signals from limited
366

367

368
embeddings derive their apparent knowledge from the textual input (i.e., NCBI descrip-
369

370
factors contribute, although the relative contribution likely varies across models depending
371
on domain specialization and corpus composition. Different LLMs encode different levels
372
of pathway knowledge, based on their pretraining corpora. At the same time, all models
373
tested in this study demonstrate strong capabilities for inferring and extracting biological
374
information even from brief textual inputs.

Despite the simplicity of our GSEA-based approach—treating each embedding dimension
376
as a potential carrier of pathway information—it has a noteworthy limitation: pathway cov-
377
erage is not invariant under rotations of the embedding space. Formally, let A be an n × p
378
embedding matrix, where n is the number of genes and p is the embedding dimension.
379
Let R be any p × p orthogonal matrix such that R⊤R = RR⊤= I. Then the transformed
380
matrix ˜A = AR preserves all pairwise relationships and geometric structure in the embed-
381
ding space. However, since GSEA is conducted on individual columns of A (i.e., specific
382
dimensions), the pathway coverage depends on the choice of R, which has infinitely many
383
possibilities. That is, A and ˜A contain the same total information, yet they may yield differ-
384
ent coverage scores. Thus, pathway information encoded by an LLM-generated embedding
385
matrix should, in principle, be measured by the maximum pathway coverage achievable
386
over all possible rotations R.
387

388

389

390
language processing, we aimed to rotate the embedding matrix to a form that yields a more
391
interpretable and sparse structure. The rotation was obtained by minimizing the following

cost function:

TP53
This gene encodes a tumor suppressor protein containing transcriptional 
activation, DNA binding, and oligomerization domains. The encoded protein 
responds to diverse cellular stresses to regulate expression of target genes, 
thereby inducing cell cycle arrest, apoptosis, senescence, DNA repair, or 
changes in metabolism. Mutations in this gene are associated with a variety 
of human cancers, including hereditary cancers such as Li-Fraumeni 
syndrome. Alternative splicing of this gene and the use of alternate promoters 
result in multiple transcript variants and isoforms. Additional isoforms have 
also been shown to result from the use of alternate translation initiation 
codons from identical transcript variants (PMIDs: 12032546, 20937277). 
[provided by RefSeq, Dec 2016]
BRCA1
This gene encodes a 190 kD nuclear phosphoprotein that plays a role in 
maintaining genomic stability, and it also acts as a tumor suppressor. The 
BRCA1 gene contains 22 exons spanning about 110 kb of DNA. The 
encoded protein combines with other tumor suppressors, DNA damage 
sensors, and signal transducers to form a large multi-subunit protein complex 
known as the BRCA1-associated genome surveillance complex (BASC). This 
gene product associates with RNA polymerase II, and through the C-terminal 
domain, also interacts with histone deacetylase complexes. This protein thus 
plays a role in transcription, DNA repair of double-stranded breaks, and 
recombination. Mutations in this gene are responsible for approximately 40% 
of inherited breast cancers and more than 80% of inherited breast and 
ovarian cancers. Alternative splicing plays a role in modulating the subcellular 
localization and physiological function of this gene. Many alternatively spliced 
transcript variants, some of which are disease-associated mutations, have 
been described for this gene, but the full-length natures of only some of these 
variants has been described. A related pseudogene, which is also located on 
chromosome 17, has been identified. [provided by RefSeq, May 2020]
A1BG
The protein encoded by this gene is a plasma glycoprotein of unknown 
function. The protein shows sequence similarity to the variable regions of 
some immunoglobulin supergene family member proteins. [provided by 
RefSeq, Jul 2008]
