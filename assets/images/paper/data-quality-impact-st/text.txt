Abstract
11
12
Spatial transcriptomic technologies enable high-throughput quantification of gene ex-
13
pression at specific locations across tissue sections, facilitating insights into the spatial orga-
14
nization of biological processes. However, high costs associated with these technologies have
15
motivated the development of deep learning methods to predict spatial gene expression from in-
16
expensive hematoxylin and eosin-stained histology images. While most efforts have focused on
17
modifying model architectures to boost predictive performance, the influence of training data
18
quality remains largely unexplored. Here, we investigate how variation in molecular and im-
19
age data quality stemming from differences in spatial transcriptomic technologies impact deep
20
learning-based gene expression prediction from histology images. To identify the aspects of
21
data quality that impact predictive performance, we conducted in silico ablation experiments,
22
which showed that increased sparsity and noise in molecular data degraded predictive perfor-
23
mance, while in silico rescue experiments via imputation provided only limited improvements
24
that failed to generalize beyond the test set. Likewise, reduced image resolution can degrade
25
predictive performance and further impacts model interpretability. We further demonstrate
26
that these data quality-driven effects are reproducible across multiple spatial transcriptomics
27
datasets and remain consistent when using alternative feature extractors and model architec-
28
tures. Overall, our results show how improving data quality provides an orthogonal strategy
29
to tuning model architecture in spatial transcriptomics-based predictive modeling, highlighting
30
the need to account for technology-specific limitations that directly impact data quality when
31
developing predictive methodologies.
32
Keywords: spatial transcriptomics, gene prediction, digital pathology, benchmark, machine learning, deep
33
learning, data-centric AI

Introduction
35
Spatial transcriptomic (ST) technologies enable the measurement of gene expression with spatial context
36
across tissue sections with varying molecular resolution and throughput. These spatial molecular measure-
37
ments are often accompanied by a hematoxylin and eosin (H&E)-stained image of the tissue section used
38
for the ST assay. Although ST experiments can cost several thousand dollars per sample, H&E-stained
39
images are relatively inexpensive and routine in clinical and research histopathology. By leveraging such
40
matched H&E-stained images and gene expression data, machine learning models can be trained to predict
41
molecular information directly from histology images. This greatly extends the utility of both archived
42
and newly generated histology images for molecular inference, potentially bypassing the need for expensive
43
ST technologies.
44
The quality of molecular data can vary widely across ST technologies, reflecting the unique tech-
45
nical constraints inherent to each technology. Imaging-based technologies (for example, STARmap [1],
46
MERFISH [2], NanoString CosMx, and 10x Xenium) provide sub-cellular resolution and typically higher
47
sensitivity but are currently limited to smaller gene panels and can suffer from optical signal overlap or
48
off-target probe-binding issues [3, 4]. In contrast, sequencing-based technologies (for example, Slide-seqV2
49
[5], DBiT-seq [6], and 10x Visium) provide transcriptome-wide coverage but at generally lower spatial reso-
50
lution and can be prone to lateral diffusion between spots, problematic spots, as well as PCR amplification
51
biases and drop-outs that yield sparse, noisy counts [7–10]. In addition to these ST technology-specific
52
limitations impacting the molecular data quality, the quality of histology images can also vary through
53
differences in staining protocols, scanner optics, image compression, and other factors that alter color bal-
54
ance, contrast, and effective pixel size across datasets [11, 12]. Although multiple sources contribute to
55
variation in data quality, their collective impact on the performance and interpretability of downstream
56
predictive models remains largely unexplored.
57
A growing body of work has applied deep learning models, varying in terms of the convolutional,
58
graph, or transformer-based architectures used, to predict gene expression from histology images using
59
ST data for training [13, 14]. At first, these approaches were almost exclusively trained and evaluated
60
on data from sequencing-based technologies, such as Visium, where predictive performance, as measured
61
by the Pearson correlation coefficient between predicted and observed gene expression, often averaged
62
around 0.3. More recent studies have begun training and evaluating models using data from imaging-
63
based technologies, such as Xenium, reporting a higher predictive performance [15, 16]. However, prediction
64
performance remains highly variable across datasets, tissue types, and models, highlighting the need for
65
further investigation into factors that impact prediction performance [17].
66
In light of this, our study assesses the impact of training data quality on the performance of gene
67
expression prediction from histology images using deep learning models. To this end, we first utilized
68
two previously published breast cancer datasets from serial tissue sections assayed by a sequencing-based
69
technology (Visium) and an imaging-based technology (Xenium). The Visium data is accompanied by the
70
10x Genomics provided high-resolution histology image and a re-scanned whole-slide H&E-stained image
71
(WSI), whereas the Xenium data includes a high-resolution WSI of the same tissue section [18]. We find
72
that when using Xenium data as the input training data compared to when using Visium data, the average
73
prediction performance across genes increased. To determine what specific data quality factors in the input
74
training data may contribute to the observed prediction performance differences between technologies, we
75
conducted a series of in silico ablation experiments. First, we performed molecular ablation experiments by
76
introducing expression sparsity and noise in the higher-performing Xenium data, which recapitulated the
77
the lower-performance observed when training on Visium data. In addition, molecular rescue experiments
78
aimed at enhancing the molecular data quality of the Visium data through gene expression imputation

Results
93
Data quality benchmarking on paired Visium and Xenium spatial transcriptomics
94
datasets
95
To investigate how ST training data quality affects gene expression prediction from histology images via
96
deep learning, we first leveraged serial section breast cancer datasets generated by Visium CytAssist and
97
Xenium (Figure 1A) [18]. Because a single tissue section cannot undergo two distinct ST assays, serial
98
sections allow us to control for biological variability and focus on technical variability across the different
99
ST technologies. Still, slight differences in the region of the tissues profiled as well as image and molecular
100
data resolution persist. Therefore, to better ensure that differences in gene expression prediction perfor-
101
mance can be attributed to the training regime rather than to confounding variation between the training
102
datasets, we standardize as many aspects of the input as possible. To this end, we first aligned the Visium
103
histology image to the Xenium histology image using STalign, bringing both datasets into a common co-
104
ordinate system (Figure 1B) [19]. This enabled the molecular data from either technology to be visualized
105
and utilized with either histology image. We then identified the shared region between the two datasets
106
and rasterized the gene expression to a common spatial resolution (Figure 1C), as demonstrated by Aihara
107
et al. [20]. By minimizing such sample-specific differences, this preprocessing enables controlled compar-
108
isons across all combinations of molecular and imaging data. Using this setup, we extracted histology
109
image patches that correspond to the rasterized molecular data and trained identical deep learning models
110
composed of a pretrained ResNet50 feature extractor, a four-layer multilayer perceptron, and a final linear
111
output layer to predict gene expression at the patch level for the 306 genes common to both ST assays
112
(Figure 1D). We evaluated model prediction performance by computing Pearson correlation coefficients
113
(PCC) and range-normalized root mean square error (rMSE) between true and predicted patch-level gene
114
expression on a held-out test set (15% of patches) and, in some cases, on an independent serial section
115
Xenium replicate dataset (Rep2) to further assess generalizability. To determine which factors of data
116
quality influence prediction performance and interpretability, we performed a series of in silico ablation
117
experiments including increasing expression sparsity and noise, applying Gaussian blur to simulate lower-
118
resolution images, as well as gene expression imputation in an attempt to remove sparsity and noise, and
119
then reassessed prediction performance as well as Grad-CAM saliency (Figure 1E).
120
Applying this approach, we directly compared the predictive performance of our deep learning model
121
when trained on each technologies’ associated histology image and corresponding molecular data (Figure 2).

For every gene, we computed the PCC between the ground-truth expression values and model predictions
123
on the held-out test set, using five independently trained models to account for training variability. We
124
found that the PCC distribution across genes is notably shifted toward higher values, indicative of better
125
prediction performance, when trained on Xenium data compared to Visium data (mean PCC = 0.715 for
126
Xenium vs. 0.519 for Visium; Figure 2A), representing an increased average prediction performance across
127
genes of approximately 38%. Likewise, in the gene-by-gene scatterplot (Figure 2B), almost all points lie
128
above the diagonal indicating that prediction performance when training on Xenium data was consistently
129
higher than with Visium data for almost all genes. Evaluating the complete distribution of ground-truth
130
and predicted expression values for held-out test set patches reveals that the PCC is not driven by outliers
131
(Supplementary Figure 1). To further ensure this trend holds across metrics, we also examined the range-
132
normalized root mean square error (rMSE) and found that gene expression predictions compared to the
133
ground-truth expression values exhibited higher normalized rMSE, indicative of worse performance, when
134
trained on Visium data compared to Xenium data (Supplementary Figure 2). Plots of observed versus
135
predicted expression for four representative genes (HDC, ANKRD30A, AHSP, GZMK) highlight varying
136
performance outcomes: Xenium-trained models outperform Visium for HDC, both models perform similarly
137
well for ANKRD30A and poorly for AHSP, while Visium-trained models slightly outperforms Xenium for
138
GZMK (Figure 2C).
139
To confirm these findings were robust to model choice, we evaluated two additional architectures.
140
The first used the same overall structure shown in Figure 1D but replaced the feature extractor with the UNI
141
histology foundational model [21], while the second used RedeHist [22], a model specifically developed for
142
gene expression prediction from histology images using spatial transcriptomics data. Using UNI resulted in
143
a slight increase in PCC for both Xenium and Visium results (mean PCC = 0.734 for Xenium vs. 0.529 for
144
Visium; Supplementary Figure 3A-B) and decrease in normalized rMSE, but the overall trend in prediction
145
performance when trained on Xenium data compared to Visium data remained the same. Using RedeHist,
146
there was a modest decrease in PCC and increase in normalized rMSE for both Xenium and Visium data
147
(mean PCC = 0.633 for Xenium vs. 0.385 for Visium; Supplementary Figure 3C-D), but again the overall
148
trend remained the same.
149
Overall, these results indicate that models trained on Xenium’s paired molecular data and histology
150
image achieve better gene expression predictive performance than those trained on the Visium molecular
151
data and its corresponding histology image. Whether this advantage arises primarily from differences in
152
molecular data quality or from image quality or both remains to be determined in our subsequent analyses.

Molecular data quality can impact spatial gene expression prediction performance
154
To disentangle the impact of molecular data quality from image data quality, we next held the image data
155
constant while swapping molecular data between technologies. We leveraged the high-resolution histology
156
image from the Visium dataset and trained two identical models: one on Visium molecular data, the
157
other on Xenium molecular data. Under these conditions, Xenium molecular data outperformed Visium
158
molecular data (mean PCC = 0.605 vs. 0.519; Figure 3A), and most genes exhibited higher PCCs when
159
trained with the Xenium molecular data (Figure 3B). We next repeated this with the WSI from the Xenium
160
dataset. Again, models trained using Xenium molecular data outperformed Visium molecular data (mean
161
PCC = 0.715 for Xenium vs. 0.492 for Visium; Figure 3C-D). These trends further held for normalized
162
rMSE (Supplementary Figure 4). This demonstrates that, irrespective of the image data, the molecular
163
data used for training has an effect on gene expression prediction performance.
164
To pinpoint what molecular data quality factors may drive these performance differences, we next
165
performed a series of in silico ablation experiments in which we systematically decrease molecular data
166
quality and evaluate its impact on prediction performance. First, we examined expression sparsity, defined
167
here as the proportion of zero counts across patches for each gene. Consistent with previous research, we
168
note that sparsity is more pronounced in the molecular data from Visium than Xenium (Supplementary
169
Figure 5).
Having confirmed Xenium data’s better performance, we increased sparsity in the patch-
170
resolution Xenium gene expression count matrix to match the sparsity in the Visium gene expression
171
count matrix. To do this, we compared corresponding patches from the Visium and Xenium datasets, and
172
wherever the Visium count matrix exhibited a value of ≤0, we set the corresponding patch in the patch-
173
resolution Xenium count matrix value to 0. We repeated this process for Visium expression thresholds
174
of ≤1, ≤5, ≤10, ≤15, and ≤20. As sparsity increased, mean PCC on the held-out test set steadily
175
declined (Figure 3E). To ensure this effect was not an artifact of the held-out test data and to access
176
model generalization on a new dataset, we repeated the evaluation on an independent Xenium serial
177
section (Rep2). Here too, we observed a similar downward trend in PCC as sparsity increased (Figure 3E).
178
Notably, training with Xenium molecular data with increased sparsity using a ≤1 threshold produced
179
comparable performance to when training with the Visium molecular data. Next, we examined expression
180
noise, defined as random fluctuations in gene counts around their true values. To simulate greater expression
181
noise, we added Poisson distributed noise into the patch-resolution Xenium count matrix across a series
182
of rate parameters (λ). As we increased λ to simulate more expression noise, mean PCC on the held-out
183
test set steadily declined (Figure 3F). We again repeated the evaluation on the independent Xenium serial
184
section (Rep2) to confirm this trend. Overall, these results suggest molecular data sparsity and noise as
185
potential drivers of decreased prediction performance.
186
Finally, given that increased sparsity and noise can degrade prediction performance, we asked
187
whether we could rescue the Visium molecular data via gene imputation.

techniques that infer and fill in missing or zeroed expression values by borrowing information from similar

Image data quality can impact spatial gene expression prediction performance
198
To isolate the effect of image data quality from molecular data quality, we held the molecular input
199
constant and swapped histology images between technologies.
First, using Visium molecular data, we
200
trained models on both the Visium high-resolution image (2000px by 1809px) and the higher resolution
201
Xenium WSI (20511px by 27587px). The resulting PCC distributions were comparable (mean PCC =
202
0.519 with Visium image vs.
0.492 with Xenium image; Figure 4A). Gene-wise comparisons of PCC
203
remained along the diagonal (Figure 4B), and normalized rMSE values were similarly low (Supplementary
204
Figure 6A), indicating that image resolution had little effect on prediction performance.
Next, using
205
Xenium molecular data, we repeated the experiment and observed a larger performance gap (mean PCC
206
= 0.605 with Visium image vs. 0.715 with Xenium image; Figure 4C). Gene-by-gene scatterplots (Figure
207
4D) and normalized rMSE comparisons (Supplementary Figure 6B) demonstrate consistent trends. As
208
such, training on higher resolution histology images can improve prediction performance depending on the
209
quality of the corresponding molecular data. Consistent with this, when using the Visium WSI (20511px by
210
27587px) instead of the CytAssist associated high-resolution image, this trend persisted, with higher PCCs
211
for models trained on the Xenium molecular data compared to the Visium molecular data (mean PCC =
212
0.678 for Xenium vs. 0.526 for Visium), accompanied by a decrease in normalized rMSE for the Xenium-
213
trained models and slight increase for the Visium-trained models (Supplementary Figure 7A-B). Likewise,
214
models trained on the Visium data showed a slight increase in PCC (from 0.519 to 0.526), accompanied
215
by a slight increase in normalized rMSE (from 0.179 to 0.181; Supplementary Figure 7C-D).
216
To pinpoint what imaging data quality factors may drive these performance differences, we simu-
217
lated lower-resolution images by applying Gaussian blur with kernels of size 5, 25, and 125 to the Xenium
218
histology images. Under matched conditions, training and testing on equally blurred images, PCC slowly
219
degraded with decreasing simulated image resolution (Figure 4E). These results suggest that image res-
220
olution has a measurable impact on gene expression prediction performance, with progressively blurred
221
images leading to decreased prediction performance.
222

223
sought to assess its impact on model interpretability. To this end, we employed Grad-CAM [26], which
224
projects prediction gradients onto convolutional feature maps to generate heatmaps that highlight the
225
image regions most influential for a given output.
This technique is especially useful because it can

227
consistent with what histopathologists may focus on, rather than background artifacts. We applied Grad-

Reproducibility of data quality effects across spatial transcriptomics technologies
236
To assess whether the observed data quality effects could be reproduced with additional spatial transcrip-
237
tomics technologies and tissues, we next applied our framework to a colon adenocarcinoma (COAD) dataset
238
comprising of serial tissue sections assayed with three spatial transcriptomics technologies: Xenium 5K,
239
VisiumHD, and CosMx 6K [27]. Xenium 5K expands the targeted panel to approximately 5000 genes,
240
compared with fewer than 400 in the original Xenium panel, although this comes with slightly reduced
241
sensitivity [28]. VisiumHD provides higher molecular resolution than Visium CytAssist, enabling mea-
242
surements at patch sizes as small as 2µm x 2µm, while also eliminating gaps between spots. CosMx 6K
243
is an imaging-based technology analogous to Xenium 5K but uses a modified two-probe chemistry rather
244
than padlock probes [29]. Using the paired H&E-stained whole-slide images and spatial transcriptomic
245

246
gene expression within each patch. We then trained five independently trained deep-learning models to
247
predict patch-level gene expression from histology images and evaluated model prediction performance
248
using PCC and rMSE on a held-out test set (15% of patches) for each dataset.
249
Consistent with our previously observed trends, prediction performance was impacted by the training
250
data, as the PCC distribution across genes is shifted toward higher values when trained on Xenium 5K data
251
compared to VisiumHD data (mean PCC = 0.568 for Xenium 5K vs. 0.434 for VisiumHD; Supplementary
252

253

254

255
the diagonal in the scatterplot (Supplementary Figure 8C), indicating consistently improved prediction
256
performance when training on Xenium 5K data relative to VisiumHD data. In contrast, models trained on
257
CosMx 6K data displayed a distinct performance distribution. The PCC distribution was largely unimodal,
258
rather than bimodal as observed for VisiumHD and Xenium 5K, with a mean PCC that fell between the
259
two technologies (mean PCC = 0.532) but accompanied by higher overall error (mean rMSE = 0.200;
260
Supplementary Figure 8A-B). Gene-wise comparisons of PCC between CosMx 6K with VisiumHD and
261

262
VisiumHD and Xenium 5K often better predicted using CosMx 6K data, whereas genes that were well
263
predicted in VisiumHD and Xenium 5K tended to have a worse prediction performance when trained on

Discussion
279
In this study, we evaluated how data quality impacts deep learning–based gene expression prediction from
280
histology images. We leveraged molecular data paired with H&E-stained images from multiple spatial
281
transcriptomic technologies across two tissue types, representing a variety of data quality features. We
282
trained identical deep learning models to predict patch-level gene expression and observed that training
283
on Xenium and Xenium 5K data can achieve quantifiably better gene expression prediction performance
284
than training on Visium, VisiumHD, or CosMx 6K data, with an average prediction performance across
285
genes increase up to 38%. These relative performance trends were consistent across alternative feature
286
extractors and model architectures. Using the breast cancer dataset to isolate the impact of molecular
287
data quality, we held the histology image constant and found that Xenium molecular data outperformed
288
Visium molecular data regardless of which image was used. In molecular ablation experiments, adding
289
noise and sparsity to Xenium molecular data steadily degraded prediction performance. Conversely, in
290
molecular rescue experiments, imputing Visium data boosted performance on the held-out test set but
291
failed to generalize to an independent Xenium replicate. Likewise, to isolate the impact of image data
292
quality, we held the molecular data constant and found Visium molecular data yielded similar prediction
293
performance on both the Visium and Xenium images, whereas Xenium molecular data benefited from
294
the higher-resolution Xenium image. Simulating lower-resolution images by applying Gaussian blur to
295
the Xenium image reduced predictive performance and obscured interpretability via Grad-CAM feature
296
maps. These trends generalized to a colon adenocarcinoma dataset containing Xenium 5K, VisiumHD, and
297
CosMx 6K, where Xenium 5K yielded higher overall prediction accuracy than VisiumHD, while CosMx 6K
298
exhibited gene-specific trade-offs driven by differences in image quality and molecular noise. Overall, these
299
findings underscore the impact of both molecular and imaging data quality in deep learning–based gene
300
expression prediction from histology images. Further, our study presents quantifiable metrics of molecular
301
and imaging data quality in terms of their sparsity, noise, and resolution that can be considered in the
302
curation of training data from ST technologies for developing gene expression prediction methodologies.
303
Despite these insights, our study has several limitations. Although our comparative analysis demon-
304
strated that training on Visium, VisiumHD, and CosMx 6K data resulted in lower gene expression pre-
305
diction performance than training on Xenium and Xenium 5K data, potentially attributable to greater
306
sparsity, noise, and other data quality issues in Visium, Xenium and Xenium 5K data can also exhibits
307
their own limitations in data quality. For example, previous research has demonstrated that imaging-based
308
technologies, like Xenium, Xenium 5K, and CosMx, can suffer from off-target probe hybridization, thereby
309
obscuring the target gene’s expression with the off-target gene’s expression [4]. Such artifacts can inflate
310
observed gene expression magnitudes, and because genes with higher mean expression are generally easier
311
to predict, may artificially boost prediction performance when models are trained on such molecular data
312
[15]. We therefore used the Off-Target Probe Tracker (OPT) with a pad-length of 10 to identify 14 of our
313
306 genes with potential protein-coding off-target genes [4] in the breast cancer data. Removing these genes
314
and retraining our models confirmed that they do not drive our observed trends or affect our conclusions
315
(Supplementary Figure 10). In the case of CosMx, elevated molecular noise or probe-related artifacts may

323
Similarly for image data quality, beyond image resolution, other features in H&E staining and
324
slide preparation can impact the performance of deep learning models. In particular, H&E staining for
325
Xenium is acquired after the in situ gene expression profiling is complete, whereas H&E staining for
326
Visium is acquired before transcriptomic capture. How such protocol variation impacts the quality and
327
generalizability of the H&E-stained images for such predictive modeling purposes remains unclear. Beyond
328
protocol variation, differences in hematoxylin versus eosin concentration, incubation time, and scanner
329
settings can alter color balance, contrast, and texture cues that deep learning models rely on. Common
330
slide artifacts such as air bubbles trapped under the coverslip, uneven tissue folds, tears or wrinkles in
331
the section, dust or debris on the slide and more can further create spurious patterns. Previous studies
332
have shown that these inconsistencies may cause models to learn batch or slide specific artifacts rather
333
than relevant tissue or cellular morphological features [30–33].
As such, we anticipate such common
334
slide artifacts that decrease the quality of the histology image would also impact prediction performance.
335
Likewise, previous studies have sought to address these issues by applying stain-normalization algorithms,
336
color augmentation pipelines, and domain-adaptive foundation models to standardize image appearance
337
across batches and technologies [11, 16, 34, 35]. Future work is needed to evaluate how much such image
338
artifacts impact prediction performance and whether such previously developed strategies are sufficient to
339
rescue poor quality images without introducing biases that limit generalizability. In cases where poor image
340
quality substantially limits predictive performance, re-scanning slides with optimized scanner settings and
341
higher objective lenses could be a practical approach to obtain higher-quality histology images for model
342
training, as demonstrated by models trained on the re-scanned whole-slide Visium image (Supplementary
343
Figure 7).
344
Much of the prior work on gene expression prediction from histology images has focused on mod-
345
ifying model architecture, exploring increasingly complex convolutional, graph, and transformer-based
346
designs in an effort to improve predictive performance. Although these architectural modifications have
347
produced incremental improvements, prediction accuracy remains highly variable across datasets, tissues,
348
and technologies, indicating that model choice alone cannot fully explain observed performance differences.
349
Consistent with this, replacing the ResNet50 feature extractor with the domain-adaptive foundation model
350
UNI resulted in modest gains in PCC and reductions in normalized rMSE, while preserving the same rel-
351
ative performance trends across datasets (Supplementary Figure 3A-B). Similarly, applying RedeHist, a
352
model specifically designed for gene expression prediction from histology images, showed the same relative
353
performance trends, though with a slightly lower overall performance, likely because predicting gene expres-
354
sion at single-cell resolution is inherently more challenging than at patch-level resolution (Supplementary
355
Figure 3C-D). Together, these results demonstrate that architectural modifications and foundation models
356
can provide incremental gains, but they are ultimately constrained by the quality of the training data,
357
emphasizing the need to prioritize data-centric approaches.
358

359
is essential for determining cell-types and their spatial organization of tissues. Further, these genes are
360
often more important for downstream biological interpretations. As such, performance for these genes may
361
be more important than performance on other broadly expressed housekeeping genes for example. Using

As with many deep learning applications, interpretability remains a key challenge. Although we can
375
demonstrate quantifiable aspects of image data quality that lead to reduced prediction performance, how

383

385

386

387
Overall, this study underscores the critical role of molecular and image data quality in spatial
388
gene expression prediction from histology images. While recent developments have focused on improving
389
modeling approaches, our findings highlight that improvements in training data quality offers an orthogonal
390
strategy to tuning model architecture in enhancing predictive modeling using spatial transcriptomics. Our
391
study further provides a comparative framework for evaluating future ST technologies to characterize
392
aspects of molecular and image data quality to guide this evolving field. Looking forward, we anticipate a
393
more holistic approach that integrates advancements in model architecture with improved generation and
394
curation of training data is likely to yield the greatest performance gains in predictive modeling with spatial
395
transcriptomics. Such a holistic approach will be important for not only improving prediction performance
396
but also enhancing interpretability to ensure reliable and safe future applications in research and clinical
397
settings.

Gene imputation, sparsity, and noise data augmentation
466
Numerous gene imputation methods exist, each representing different functional classes [38].
For this
467
analysis, we selected three methods: KNN [23], MAGIC [24], and SCVI [25].
468
KNN-smoothing smooths gene expression by identifying nearest neighbors based on partially smoothed
469
and variance-stabilized expression profiles and aggregating their transcript counts. For this analysis, the
470
number of neighbors was set to k=50, and d=20 top principal components were used to determine the
471
nearest neighbors during each smoothing step. Patch 1587 within the dataset was excluded before applying
472
KNN-smoothing and reintroduced afterward due to insufficient gene counts. The raw UMI counts were
473
used as input, as recommended by the authors.
474
MAGIC (Markov Affinity-based Graph Imputation of Cells) smooths gene expression by employing
475
a graph-based approach, where nodes represent individual cells and edges encode similarities between them,
476
and diffusing gene expression information across this graph. For this analysis, the number of neighbors
477
was set to knn = 50, and the diffusion operator’s power, t, was set to ”auto.” All other parameters were
478
kept at their default values. Log-transformed UMI counts with a pseudo-count of 1 were used as input, as
479
recommended by the authors.
480

481
scriptomic gene expression using a neural network. The neural network was trained using the Chromium
482
5’ Gene Expression data from another serial section of the breast cancer dataset, along with 80% of the

spatial data. All parameters were maintained at their default values, and the model was trained for a

Neural network architectures and parameters
498
For image feature extraction, we used a pretrained ResNet50 model with the classification layer removed.
499
The input consisted of extracted patches from histology images, sized 250x250x3 which were resized to
500
the standard ResNet50 input size of 224x224x3. The patches were normalized per channel using the mean
501
and standard deviation values from the pre-trained ResNet50 dataset.
To enhance model robustness,
502
the patches were subjected to random horizontal, vertical, and 90-degree rotations. The output of the
503
ResNet50 model was the last layer of the model, a feature vector of size 2048, representing extracted
504
image features. Once image features were extracted for each patch, we passed them through a four-layer
505
multilayer perceptron (MLP) to both reduce dimensionality and learn the features most predictive of gene
506
expression. Each MLP layer consisted of a linear transformation, batch normalization, ReLU activation,
507
and 20% dropout. The final output is predicted gene expression for all 306 genes per patch. For training,
508
mean-squared error (MSE) loss was used alongside the Adam optimizer [39], with a learning rate of 0.001
509
and weight decay of 0.00001. During training, a batch size of 64 was used, and each model was trained
510
for 150 epochs. The datasets were divided into training (75%), validation (10%), and test (15%) sets. All
511
results shown are on the test sets except the plotted predicted gene expression (Figure 2C) which is on the
512
full dataset. Training was performed on an NVIDIA RTX 6000 Ada Generation GPU.
513
To evaluate the impact of an alternative feature extractor, we replaced the pretrained ResNet50
514
with the UNI domain-adaptive foundation model [21].
Histology patches were normalized and resized
515
according to the UNI preprocessing protocol, and image features were extracted from the final embedding
516
layer of the pretrained UNI model. These UNI-derived feature embeddings were then passed through the
517
same four-layer MLP used in the ResNet50 model to predict patch-level gene expression. All other data
518
augmentation strategies, training procedures, hyperparameters, dataset splits, and evaluation protocols
519
were identical to those in the ResNet50-based models.
520

521

RedeHist takes histology images,
522

523
responding spatial coordinates and cell-type annotations. As the RedeHist GitHub repository provides
524

527
