Abstract 
Spatial transcriptomics has revolutionized biological research but is limited by trade-
offs between spatial resolution and gene coverage. Here we present SpaFoundation, 
a versatile visual foundational model with 80 million trainable parameters, pre-trained 
on 1.84 million histological image patches to learn general-purpose imaging 
representations. Using histology images alone, SpaFoundation accurately infers 
spatial gene expression and demonstrates strong robustness and transferability 
across tasks such as super-resolution inference, tumor detection, and spatial 
clustering.  
 
Main 
Spatial transcriptomics (ST) has emerged as a powerful technology for capturing both 
gene expression and spatial localization information within tissues1–4, enabling 
researchers to characterize spatial gene expression patterns, study cell-cell 
communications, and resolve the spatiotemporal order of cellular development5–8. ST 
technologies can be roughly divided into two categories, including sequencing-based 
technologies, such as STARmap9, 10x Visium, SLIDE-seqV210, and Stereo-seq11, and 
imaging-based technologies, such as DNA seqFISH+12, MERSCOPE13, and 
Nanostring CosMx14. These methods differ in their spatial resolution and gene 
coverage. Sequencing-based methods are capable of measuring genome-wide gene 
expression but face lower spatial resolution. By contrast, Imaging-based methods have 
higher spatial resolution but are limited to lower throughput. These limitations 
constraint their broad applicability. Alternatively, computational approaches can bridge 
the gap between spatial resolution and gene coverage by inferring genome-wide 
expressions from histological images15,16.

visual-language tasks. However, these methods cannot be directly applicable to spatial 
transcriptomics due to inherent differences in image characteristics.  
In this study, we present SpaFoundation (Fig. 1a), a visual foundation model to 
extract general-purpose histological imaging features for spatial transcriptomics 
analysis. Building on a teacher–student architecture, SpaFoundation incorporated self-
distillation and masked image modeling (MIM) to enhance the learning of high-level 
semantic and local structural features. We pre-trained SpaFoundation on HEST-1K-3 
dataset (Fig. 1b), which comprises 1.84 million image patches from 1,113 samples 
across 26 tissue organs. SpaFoundation outperformed traditional methods with 
minimal or zero-shot fine-tuning across four downstream tasks, including spatial gene 
expression prediction, high-resolution gene expression inference, tumor detection, and 
spatial clustering (Fig. 1c). In addition, we conducted an ablation study to demonstrate 
the effectiveness of the foundation model in extracting histological imaging features 
(full experimental details are in the Supplementary Information).

To further assess SpaFoundation’s ability to extract biologically meaningful features 
from tissue morphology, we applied it to identify tumor detection in spatial tissue 
images. For this evaluation, we used a dataset from He et al. (2020) containing 68 
tissue sections from 23 breast cancer patients. The pre-trained SpaFoundation model 
was fine-tuned on this dataset for one epoch, with five patients held out for testing and 
the remaining used for training. We compared SpaFoundation against ResNet5031 and 
VGG1632, two widely used convolutional neural networks that were pretrained in a 
supervised manner on the large-scale natural image dataset ImageNet-1K. These two 
methods are commonly employed across various computer vision tasks.  
Visually, the tumor segmentation maps generated by SpaFoundation showed 
greater alignment with ground truth annotations, whereas both ResNet50 and VGG16 
frequently misclassified non-tumor regions as tumor regions (Fig. 2e). To quantitatively 
assess performance, we calculated accuracy (ACC), F1 score (F1), and area under 
ROC curve (AUC) using manual annotations. SpaFoundation consistently 
outperformed ResNet50 and VGG16 in terms of both ACC and F1 (Fig. 2f, g). 
SpaFoundation achieved an AUC of 0.94 across all test samples, outperforming 
ResNet and VGG by 4.16% and 1.55%, respectively (Fig. 2h). These results highlight 
the advantages of SpaFoundation over conventional vision networks. On one hand, 
they demonstrate the strong capability of pretrained foundation models to learn 
biologically meaningful features from histological images. On the other hand, 
SpaFoundation, as a domain-specific and unsupervised pretrained model, outperforms 
models trained in a supervised manner on general-purpose image data, underscoring 
the necessity of developing large-scale foundation models tailored to domain-specific 
biomedical applications. Overall, these findings confirm the scalability and 
effectiveness of SpaFoundation for tumor detection in spatial tissue images.

Finally, we further validated SpaFoundation’s broad applicability to spatial clustering.

HER2+ dataset, and compared SpaFoundation with state-of-the-art methods, 
including Scanpy33, SpaGCN34, and stLearn35. This task was designed as a zero-shot 
scenario where spot-level imaging representations were extracted from the pretrained 
SpaFoundation model without any fine-tuning. These representations were then 
subjected to PCA for dimensionality reduction, followed by Leiden clustering.

distinguished cancer from non-cancer regions, it failed to separate invasive from in situ 
cancer, and SpaGCN also mixed these two cancer types. Among non-cancer regions, 
SpaFoundation was the only method that accurately delineated adipose tissue with 
minimal noise. In the C1 sample, annotated into three regions, namely invasive cancer,

Conclusion 
In summary, we present SpaFoundation, a vision foundational model with 80 million 
trainable parameters for the analysis of spatial transcriptomics (ST). SpaFoundation 
has important implications for real-world applications, particularly in addressing the 
limitations of current ST technologies, which are often constrained by high cost, limited 
resolution, and low throughput. Notably, by leveraging histological images alone, 
SpaFoundation enables the inference of spatial gene expression profiles, identification 
of tumor regions, and delineation of spatial domains, offering a cost-efficient and 
scalable alternative to experimental profiling. The key advantage of SpaFoundation 
lies in its ability to learn general-purpose, morphology-aware representations through 
large-scale pretraining on 1.84 million histological images. This enables strong 
performance across diverse downstream ST analysis tasks without the need for 
extensive fine-tuning. Through analysis of various downstream applications, 
SpaFoundation demonstrates remarkable transferability and robustness across 
multiple tissues and scenarios. Moreover, we have shown that SpaFoundation 
consistently outperforms conventional methods trained on limited, task-specific data, 
underscoring the powerful potential of foundation model in capturing morphological 
features for biological discovery. Collectively, SpaFoundation open new avenues for 
image-guided spatial omics and holds potential to advance histology-driven 
computational analyses in spatial biology.

Methods 
Pretraining Dataset for SpaFoundation 
We adopt the HEST-1K spatial transcriptomics dataset, curated by Guillaume et al., as 
the pretraining dataset for SpaFoundation. HEST-1K encompasses spatial 
transcriptomics data generated using three distinct technologies, i.e., Spatial 
Transcriptomics, Xenium, and Visium (including Visium HD), with each sample paired 
with a corresponding histological image. The dataset includes 1,229 samples from 153 
public or internal cohorts, spanning two species: Homo sapiens (47.2%) and Mus 
musculus (52.8%). After tissue detection and segmentation, HEST-1K yields 
approximately 2.1 million expression–morphology pairs (each patch sized 224×224 
pixels) and 76.4 million nuclei. Notably, three datasets used in the downstream tasks, 
i.e., HER2+, cSCC, and HBCIS, are excluded from pre-training. The remaining subset, 
referred to as HEST-1K-3, includes 1,113 samples across 26 distinct tissue types. 
SpaFoundation model architecture 
SpaFoundation is pretrained using the iBOT36 framework to learn generalizable 
pathological representations useful for spatial transcriptomics analysis (Fig. 1a). To this 
end, we employ a ViT-B/16-based teacher-student network that learns patch-level 
representations by encoding paired local and global crops, as well as masked global 
crops. The model is optimized through two complementary objectives, i.e., self-
distillation and masked image modeling (MIM). The self-distillation component 
minimizes cross-entropy loss between the [CLS] tokens of different crops from the 
same pathological image, capturing high-level semantic information. The MIM branch 
reconstructs the patch tokens learned from the original and masked global crops, 
enabling the model to preserve fine-grained local structure features. By jointly 
optimizing the self-distillation and MIM objectives, SpaFoundation facilitates 
comprehensive modeling of multi-level semantics in pathological images, resulting in 
biologically meaningful and robust feature representations for various downstream 
tasks. Next, we will elaborate on the components in detail. 
Self-distillation for learning high-level semantic features. Tissue structures 
often exhibit significant variability in shape, size, and orientation in the 
pathological images, posing significant challenges for learning representations 
that can be generalizable for diverse downstream analysis. To address these 
challenges and enhance semantic feature learning, we incorporate a self-
distillation mechanism into the SpaFoundation model backbone. This 
mechanism is designed to capture high-level semantic features by encouraging 
consistency across multiple views of the same image. Specifically, given an 
input image patch, we generate m global crops (with crop ratio ranging from 
32% to 100%) and n local crops (with crop ratio ranging from 5% to 32%) 
through random cropping and image augmentation operations. Although the 
local and global crops are derived from the same image, they capture different 
structure features due to variations in position and scale. To further increase 
the learning robustness, we create masked global crops by dividing each global 
crop into non-overlapping image patches and randomly masking some

SpaFoundation pretraining details 
In this study, the SpaFoundation model was trained for a total of 10 epochs. 
SpaFoundation was trained using a distributed training strategy across 8 Tesla 
V100 NVlink GPUs (each with 32GB of memory), enabling parallel computation 
with a batch size of 64 per GPU. The model architecture was based on Vision 
Transformer (ViT), specifically the vit_base variant. We employed the AdamW 
optimizer, initialized with a learning rate of 0.0002 and dynamically adjusted 
using a cosine annealing scheduler. To improve training efficiency and stability, 
we implemented mixed precision training, gradient clipping, and initially frozen 
the final layer to facilitate better convergence. For data augmentation, we 
generated two global views and ten local views for each input image patch. 
Additionally, standard image preprocessing, including random cropping, color 
jitter, and horizontal flipping, were applied. 
Evaluation 
In this study, we evaluated SpaFoundation’s performance across four tasks, 
including spatial gene expression prediction, high-resolution gene expression 
inference, tumor region detection, and spatial clustering. To better assess 
model performance, we used evaluation metrics tailored to each task. 
Specifically, both spatial gene expression prediction and high-resolution gene 
expression inference tasks were evaluated using the Pearson correlation 
coefficient (PCC), which measures the linear correlation between raw and 
predicted gene expression values. For tumor region detection, accuracy (ACC) 
and F1 score were employed, along with receiver operating characteristic (ROC) 
curves and the area under the ROC curve (AUC). In the spatial clustering, the 
Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) and Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) were 
used to evaluate the agreement between known and predicted labels. These 
evaluation metrics allowed for a comprehensive analysis and comparison of 
model capability across different tasks.

comprises 12 tissue sections from 4 patients, while the HER2+ cohort includes 
36 sections from 8 patients. To evaluate SpaFoundation’s robustness, we 
designed two prediction scenarios, i.e., highly expressed genes (HEG) 
expression prediction and highly variable genes (HVG) expression prediction.

Tumor region detection task. We next assessed the effectiveness of 
SpaFoundation in detecting tumor regions in the cancer tissue samples using

patients were selected as the test cohort, while the remaining patients were 
used for training. The test patients were specifically chosen to ensure that each 
molecular subtype was represented by at least one patient.

training set to extract generalizable pathological representations. These 
representations were then input into a classifier to predict whether a given patch 
belonged to a tumor region. The classifier comprises two sequential modules, 
each composed of a layer normalization followed by a linear transformation. 
The model was trained in a supervised manner using binary cross-entropy loss.  
For assessing SpaFoundation’s ability in extracting meaningful pathological 
representations, we compared it with two popular baseline models, i.e., 
ResNet_in1k (ResNet-50)31 and VGG_in1k (VGG-16)32. Both were initialized

Spatial clustering task. We also examined the capability of SpaFoundation on 
spatial clustering task. For this task, we used the same HER2+ dataset as the 
spatial gene expression prediction task. This dataset includes 36 tissue 
sections from 8 patients. For each patient, one section was manually annotated 
by expert pathologists, resulting in 8 annotated sections (i.e., A1, B1, C1, D1, 
E1, F1, G2, H1). The spatial clustering task was designed as a zero-shot setting. 
Notably, taking as inputs tissue image patches, we extracted patch-level 
representations using the pre-trained SpaFoundation model without any fine-
tuning. These representations were then subjected to dimensionality reduction 
via PCA, followed by unsupervised clustering using the Leiden algorithm38.   
SpaFoundation fine-tuning details 
SpaFoundation was fine-tuned for 20 (25 for HER2+), 20, 1 epochs for the 
spatial gene expression prediction, high-resolution spatial gene expression 
prediction, tumor region detection tasks, respectively. The spatial clustering 
task was evaluated in a zero-shot setting and thus no fine-tuning was performed.  
The spatial gene expression prediction and spatial clustering tasks were 
executed on an NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU, while the high-resolution prediction 
and tumor detection tasks were run on an NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU. All models 
were trained with a batch size of 64 using the Adam optimizer with an initial 
learning rate of 1×10⁻⁵. For the gene expression prediction tasks, a StepLR 
learning rate scheduler was applied, reducing the learning rate by a factor of 
0.9 every 50 epochs. 
To enhance robustness to orientation and spatial variation, online data 
augmentation was applied during training. Each image was subjected to 
random horizontal and vertical flips with a 50% probability, as well as random 
rotations selected from {−90°, 0°, 90°, 180°}. For automated resolution search, 
the resolution was scanned from 0.1 to 3.0 in steps of 0.01. The number of 
neighbors for k-nearest neighbor (KNN) graph construction was fixed at 6. 
Baseline methods 
To validate the effectiveness of SpaFoundation, we compared SpaFoundation 
with HistoGene20, His2ST19, THItoGene21, BLEEP22, and TRIPLEX23 in the 
spatial gene expression prediction task. For high-resolution gene expression 
prediction, SpaFoundation was compared with ST-Net17. To evaluate the ability 
of SpaFoundation in learning biologically meaningful representations, we 
compared SpaFoundation with RestNet31 and VGG32 for tumor region detection. 
To validate the performance of SpaFoundation in the spatial clustering, we

His2ST. His2ST is an extended version of HistoGene that integrates a graph

THItoGene. THItoGene is a deep learning framework that integrates dynamic 
convolution and capsule networks to adaptively capture latent molecular 
signals from histological images. We followed the default parameter settings, 
with the batch size and number of training epochs set to 1 and 200, respectively. 
To ensure reproducibility, we applied training data augmentation strategy, 
implemented deterministic sample sorting, and fixed the random seed. 
BLEEP. BLEEP is a bimodal embedding model that predicts spatial gene 
expression from H&E-stained histology images using contrastive learning. 
During inference, BLEEP provides three strategies. We adopted the simple 
averaging method with $K = 50$, which demonstrated the best performance in 
the original study. The model was trained using two GPUs with a batch size of 
64. All other settings followed the original configuration reported in the BLEEP 
paper, including training for 150 epochs and using ResNet-50 as the image 
encoder. 
TRIPLEX. TRIPLEX is a deep learning framework designed to predict spatial 
gene expression from Whole Slide Images (WSIs). As the model provides pre-
extracted image features, we did not apply online image augmentation during 
inference. To ensure consistency with other baseline models, we disabled two 
additional configurations originally used in TRIPLEX: test set validation and 
expression smoothing.
