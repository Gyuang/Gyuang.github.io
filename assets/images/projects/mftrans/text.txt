Abstract
Accurate tumor segmentation in whole slide image (WSI)
is essential for histopathological diagnosis and research,
but the traditional manual analysis is labor-intensive and
prone to variability.
Furthermore, many artificial mod-
els focus on specific magnification images, limiting the
detailed information available for segmentation.
To ad-
dress these challenges, we propose MFTrans, a novel multi-
resolution fusion transformer with a CNN-based architec-
ture designed for efficient tumor segmentation in WSI. In-
spired by the diagnostic procedures of expert pathologists,
MFTrans integrates both high- and low-magnification im-
ages, capturing detailed local features and broader con-
textual relationships through a dual-branch architecture.
The model employs a global token transformer and cross-
attention mechanism to fuse hierarchical features from dual
branches to improve segmentation performance. We eval-
uate MFTrans on three real-world WSI datasets: Came-
lyon16, PAIP2019, and Catholic Uijeongbu St. Mary’s hos-
pital dataset, demonstrating its superior segmentation per-
formance over state-of-the-art methods in balanced and im-
balanced setups. These results highlight MFTrans’s effec-
tiveness in medical image analysis and its generalizabil-
ity across different datasets, making it a robust tool for
automated cancer diagnostics.
Our code is available at
https://github.com/aimed-gist/MFTrans.

2. Related Work
CNN-based methods For the field of medical image
segmentation, CNNs with a U-shaped structure, commonly
known as U-Net [32], and its extensions showed superior
performance [2, 25, 37, 39].
This success was attributed
to the skip connections that allowed the network to com-
bine low-level spatial information with high-level contex-
tual information, thereby enhancing segmentation accuracy
and performance. Zhou et al. Proposed U-Net++ utilized
nested and dense skip pathways to enhance feature propa-
gation further and reduce the semantic gap between the en-
coder and decoder [43]. Attention U-Net integrated atten-
tion mechanisms into the U-Net architecture to focus on the
most relevant features [29]. Recently, U-Net 3+ was pro-
posed to incorporate full-scale skip connections and multi-
scale feature aggregation [18]. This model significantly im-
proved segmentation accuracy while maintaining computa-
tional efficiency.
Transformer-based methods Based on the success of
ViTs architecture, researchers applied it to medical image
segmentation [9, 11, 14, 20, 24, 30]. Compared with CNN-
based models, ViT models perceived the entire image as a
sequence of patches, making them adept at understanding
overall tissue morphology and the spatial distribution of tu-
mor cells across the slide. TransUNet utilized a ViT encoder
within a U-shaped structure [7], while ViT-V-Net extended
this approach to volumetric medical data [6]. The Medi-
cal Transformer (MedT) employed a gated axial-attention
mechanism to extract high-level global information and
fine-grained local details separately [36]. SwinUNet and
Swin-UNETR learned hierarchical feature representation
through shifted windows with local attention [4,16]. These
methods demonstrated the potential of ViTs in capturing
complex patterns and enhancing segmentation accuracy in
medical imaging. However, fully ViT-based models require
high computational costs and large labeled datasets, which
are not suitable for the medical domain.
Dual-branch methods To overcome high computational
costs and the need for large labeled datasets, researchers
proposed dual-branched approaches integrating CNNs and
transformers. These methods aimed to combine the local
feature extraction capabilities of CNNs with the global con-
text modeling of transformers, enhancing the overall per-
formance of medical image segmentation. TransFuse is an
early dual-branch model combining CNNs for local fea-
ture extraction and transformers for capturing global con-
text and fusing CNN and transformer features with vari-
ous pooling attention [41]. DHUnet also utilized a dual
branch approach with CNNs and transformers to extract
features separately [38]. However, it merged feature rep-
resentation via transposed convolution feature concatena-
tion at multiple levels to enhance segmentation accuracy.
Although these approaches showed improved segmentation
performance, existing methods only focused on a specific
resolution of WSI that contained far fewer details.

Classifier block
In the last stage, we add an auxiliary network (i.e., the clas-

of our model [34]. We utilize an attention-based MLP to
classify whether each window originates from a tumor or

phase. The detailed description is presented in the Supple-

3.4. Fusion branch
3.4.1
Token fusion block
In order to integrate multi-resolution WSI, we propose the
token fusion block based on a cross-attention mechanism
and apply it to each stage, as shown in Fig. 3-(c). Briefly,
we first assess globally important local features by using a
cross-attention mechanism, where the global token is used
as the query, and the local feature serves as the key and
value, denoted as “cross attention” in Fig. 3-(c). Next, we
concatenate it with the global feature and integrate them
based on inverted bottleneck structure [33], which consists
of a 5x5 convolution, LN, PConv for channel expansion, a
GELU activation function, and another PConv to compress
the channels again, denoted as “Fuse block” in Fig. 3-(c).
Finally, the output of the Fuse block is concatenated with
the result from the previous Image decoder block, described
in below.
3.5. Image decoder block
Inspired by DHUnet [38], we implement an image de-

used in subsequent stages to account for the dimensional
differences between each stage, as shown in Fig. 3-(b). For
instance, in the first stage, the image decoder transforms
the shape from ht × wt × Ct to 4ht × 4wt × Ct. In the
following stages, the shape changes from ht × wt × Ct to
2ht × 2wt × 1

rate additional auxiliary segmentation losses from interme-

fier block in the final stage. During training, these auxiliary
losses are combined with the total loss, though they are not
used during the inference phase. The overall training loss is

4. Experiments
In this section, we conduct empirical experiments to pro-
vide the promise of integrating multi-resolution based on
the dual approach in tumor segmentation. The dataset and
experimental settings details are described in Sec. 4.1. In
section Sec. 4.2, we present the three experiment results.
First, we present the results of three experiments. First,
we evaluate the segmentation performance of the proposed
method for breast cancer metastases in lymph nodes us-
ing the Camelyon 16 dataset. Next, we evaluate general-
ization of our model across different dataset (i.e., Catholic
Uijeongbu St. Mary’s hospital dataset). Finally, we demon-
strates robusteness of our model using data from entirely
different organ (i.e., PAIP dataset). Finally, We conduct ab-
lation studies to validate our method in section Sec. 4.3.
4.1. Experimental setups
4.1.1
Data description
The Camelyon16 dataset The Camelyon16 dataset [3] is
part of the Camelyon Grand Challenge 2016, which fo-
cused on the detection of metastases in sentinel lymph
node whole-slide images from two independent datasets.
It includes 400 H&E-stained WSIs (i.e., 270 training im-
ages and 130 testing images). The images are annotated
with lesion-level ground truth, providing a robust dataset
for evaluating algorithms for metastasis detection.
The
high resolution and detailed annotations of the Camelyon16
dataset make it an ideal benchmark for testing the effective-
ness of segmentation algorithms in identifying metastatic
regions.
Catholic Uijeongbu St. Mary’s hospital dataset We in-
clude a total of 83 WSIs of lymph nodes from 36 patients
undergoing either radical mastectomy or breast-conserving
surgery with sentinel lymph node biopsy at Catholic Ui-
jeongbu St. Mary’s hospital between January 2023 and De-
cember 2023. Of these, 59 WSIs from 22 patients show
evidence of metastatic carcinoma, while 24 WSIs from 14
patients exhibit normal lymph node histology. The sizes
of the metastatic lesions range from 0.03 mm to 15.4 mm.
pecifically, 49 WSIs are classified as macrometastatic (> 2
mm), 8 WSIs as micrometastatic (> 0.2 mm and ≤2.0 mm
or ≤0.2 mm and > 200 cells), aand 2 WSIs as containing
isolated tumor cells (≤0.2 mm and ≤200 cells). An expert

Implementation details
1) CNN-based methods: We compare our method with
CNN-based models, such as Unet 2) Transformer-based
methods: Since the ViT-based model successfully seg-
ments objects both natural images and medical images,
we compare MFTrans with transformer-based models, in-

4.3. Ablation Studies
Effect of sample imbalance varying proportion of tumor
We evaluate the effect of varying tumor-to-normal tissue
ratios on segmentation performance. As shown in Fig. 7,
our method outperforms state-of-the-art models, particu-
larly when the dataset contains more than twice as many
normal patches as tumor patches. This demonstrates the
advantage of our approach in real-world scenarios (i.e., nor-
mal:tumor=5:1), where datasets typically have a higher pro-
portion of normal tissue compared to tumor regions.
Effect of Global token number The number of global to-
kens plays a crucial role in the model’s ability to capture
integrated context within the global branch and in selecting
important features from the local branch during fusion. As
shown in Tab. 5, using too many tokens can degrade perfor-
mance, while 9 tokens provide the best results. Fewer to-
kens struggle to capture sufficient global context, whereas
increasing model complexity and leading to overfitting on
less relevant features. Additional experimental results are
provided in the Supplementary S4.
