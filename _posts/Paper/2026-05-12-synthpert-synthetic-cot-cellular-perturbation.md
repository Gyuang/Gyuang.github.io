## TL;DR
- SynthPert LoRA-finetunes an 8B DeepSeek-R1 student on **critic-filtered synthetic chain-of-thought traces** generated by o4-mini, rather than on raw (cell, perturbation, gene) → label tuples.
- On PerturbQA differential-expression prediction it reaches **0.70–0.79 AUROC across four cell lines, beating SUMMER by +0.10–0.21** using the same backbone as the data-only SFT control.
- Three-class accuracy is **89% (87% on held-out RPE1)** with a single 8B student that outperforms its o4-mini teacher (52%) — but the headline is dominated by the 85%-majority "not DE" class, and UP-class recall is only 0.14.

## Motivation

Predicting whether a CRISPRi knockdown drives a target gene up, down, or leaves it unchanged is core to virtual-cell pipelines, yet deep models (GEARS, scGPT) often fail to beat trivial baselines on unseen contexts (Ahlmann-Eltze & Theis, 2024). LLMs carry broad biological priors from pretraining, but existing LLM-based approaches use them only as embedding generators (GenePT) or as inference-time RAG reasoners (SUMMER) — *none of them learn perturbation reasoning end-to-end*. SUMMER additionally sidesteps the natural three-way classification by decomposing it into two binary subtasks, which is not how a wet-lab researcher would use the model.

SynthPert targets this gap: a single LLM that performs joint three-class prediction with explicit biological reasoning, trained on **synthetic CoT** rather than scarce experimental tuples. The medical-AI motivation is in-silico CRISPRi screening for target discovery and therapeutic prioritization.

## Core Innovation

The load-bearing idea is **reasoning-distillation, not label-distillation**. The frontier teacher (o4-mini) is asked to write a mechanistic rationale for a (cell type, perturbation, gene, ground-truth label) tuple. A separate critic LLM grades only the `<think>` block on a 5-point scale, and only `excellent` traces survive — about **2% of the candidate pool, ~14k traces**. The 8B student is then LoRA-SFT'd to imitate the `<think>…</think><answer>…</answer>` strings end-to-end. Supervision is on *plausible mechanism*, not on labels; the authors' claim is that this is what transfers across cell types.

**Honest take.** C1 is a real, large, consistent margin against a fair same-backbone control — that is the result that matters. Everything downstream of C1 is rhetorically oversold: the 89% three-class accuracy is a majority-class story masking a 0.14 UP-recall, the cross-cell-type claim rests on a single fold with no seed variance, and **no table in the paper reports error bars or significance tests**. The "student beats teacher" framing is at least partly a calibration trade, not necessarily a reasoning win.

## Method & Architecture

![SynthPert workflow](/assets/images/paper/synthpert/page_002.png)
*Figure 1: SynthPert workflow — o4-mini generates chain-of-thought rationales conditioned on (perturbation, gene, ground-truth outcome); a critic LLM keeps only "excellent" traces; the 8B DeepSeek-R1 student is LoRA-SFT'd on the filtered synthetic CoTs.*

**Task formulation.** Learn $f: (\text{cell type}, \text{perturbation}, \text{gene}) \mapsto \{\text{up}, \text{down}, \text{not DE}\}$, parameterized by an LLM that emits `<think>…</think><answer>…</answer>` tokens.

1. **Base model.** DeepSeek-R1 distilled from Llama-3.1-8B (8B params). Fine-tuned once and evaluated on both binary subtasks and the three-class task — no per-task heads.
2. **Synthetic CoT generation (two variants; only #2 is the final method).**
   - *Approach 1 — Prediction + Explanation:* o4-mini sees only the tuple, must both predict and justify; keep only correctly-predicted traces.
   - *Approach 2 — Explanation from Outcome (used):* o4-mini receives the ground-truth label *in the prompt* and writes a mechanistic rationale consistent with it. A critic LLM rates the reasoning block (excellent / good / average / bad / terrible); only `excellent` survives.
3. **Quality filtering.** Critic considers pathway relevance, logical flow, cell-type-specific context — not the final label. Roughly 2% of candidate traces (~14k) pass.
4. **Supervised fine-tuning.** LoRA SFT on the full `<think>…</think><answer>…</answer>` string with standard next-token cross-entropy. AdamW lr $1\times 10^{-4}$, per-GPU batch size 4, 5 warmup steps, max seq length 2048, 50 epochs, BF16, one A100 80GB, ~1.5 GPU-hours, unsloth.
5. **Baseline isolation.** A second model ("LLM + SFT on Data") is trained with identical hyperparameters on raw (input → label) tuples without explanations. This is what isolates the contribution of *reasoning traces* from mere exposure to perturbation data.
6. **Inference prompt.** System prompt mandates reasoning inside `<think>` tags and a final tag of {upregulated, downregulated, not differentially expressed} inside `<answer>`. For the direction-only subtask, the prompt forbids "not DE".

The Approach-2 design choice deserves a flag: ground-truth labels appear in the *CoT-generation prompt*, so synthetic traces are post-hoc rationalizations rather than de novo reasoning. The paper does not test whether the student picks up label-leaking stylistic shortcuts.

## Experimental Results

**Dataset.** PerturbQA (Wu et al., 2024), derived from Replogle 2022 / Nadig 2024 Perturb-seq screens. Four cell lines: K562, Jurkat, HepG2, RPE1 (RPE1 is the held-out line for cross-cell-type transfer). 75/25 splits. ~85% of genes are "not DE".

**Binary AUROC on PerturbQA (single run per cell, single seed):**

| Task | Model | K562 | RPE1 | HepG2 | Jurkat |
|------|-------|------|------|-------|--------|
| Differential Expression | GEARS | 0.54 | 0.50 | 0.48 | 0.51 |
| | scGPT | 0.52 | 0.52 | 0.48 | 0.51 |
| | GenePT-prot | 0.57 | 0.56 | 0.54 | 0.55 |
| | SUMMER | 0.60 | 0.58 | 0.61 | 0.58 |
| | LLM + SFT on Data | 0.59 | 0.59 | 0.60 | 0.60 |
| | **SynthPert** | **0.70** | **0.78** | **0.74** | **0.79** |
| Direction of Change | GEARS | 0.64 | 0.60 | 0.52 | 0.51 |
| | GenePT-prot | 0.57 | 0.57 | 0.55 | 0.58 |
| | SUMMER | 0.62 | 0.64 | 0.65 | **0.66** |
| | LLM + SFT on Data | 0.47 | 0.55 | 0.55 | 0.50 |
| | **SynthPert** | **0.65** | **0.73** | **0.72** | 0.65 |

Note that the data-only SFT control (same backbone, same hyperparameters, no synthetic CoT) is roughly on par with SUMMER on DE and *worse* than SUMMER on Direction-of-Change — the gap between SynthPert and "LLM + SFT on Data" cleanly isolates the contribution of synthetic reasoning traces.

**Three-class evaluation, all four cell lines pooled (Table 3):**

| Model | NotDE F1 | UP F1 (P / R) | DOWN F1 (P / R) | Accuracy |
|-------|----------|---------------|-----------------|----------|
| Base LLM (DeepSeek-R1 8B) | 0.21 | 0.11 (0.07 / 0.32) | 0.14 (0.08 / 0.38) | 0.15 |
| SFT on data | 0.69 | 0.13 (0.08 / 0.29) | 0.18 (0.12 / 0.35) | 0.52 |
| o4-mini (teacher) | 0.68 | 0.20 (0.12 / 0.62) | 0.18 (0.15 / 0.23) | 0.52 |
| **SynthPert** | **0.95** | 0.22 (0.49 / **0.14**) | **0.53** (0.52 / 0.53) | **0.89** |

The bolded UP-recall (0.14) is what to look at when reading the 89% accuracy claim. The model is highly selective on UP — when it predicts UP it is right about half the time (P=0.49) — but it predicts UP very rarely. o4-mini, by contrast, has UP-recall 0.62 at P=0.12. These are calibration regimes, not strict orderings of "reasoning ability."

**Cross-cell-type generalization, RPE1 held out (Table 4):**

| Model | NotDE F1 | UP F1 | DOWN F1 | Accuracy |
|-------|----------|-------|---------|----------|
| Base LLM | 0.22 | 0.09 | 0.14 | 0.15 |
| SFT on data | 0.58 | 0.09 | 0.14 | 0.40 |
| o4-mini | 0.68 | 0.13 | 0.21 | 0.52 |
| **SynthPert** | **0.94** | **0.21** | **0.45** | **0.87** |

**Ablations and qualitative findings (Discussion / Appendix).**
- *Class rebalancing* to ⅓-⅓-⅓ yielded "roughly the same" performance — no table.
- *EnrichR pathway injection at inference* hurt AUROC by 0.07; the paper attributes this to context-length saturation, but does not run a context-length control.
- *Manual factuality audit* (n=17, author-graded): correct-prediction CoTs averaged 0.92 factuality per clause vs 0.76 for incorrect-prediction CoTs.
- *Approach 1 vs Approach 2:* similar performance; Approach 2 is more data-efficient. Numbers in supplementary, not in main text.

## Limitations

**Author-acknowledged.**
- Class imbalance distorts minority-class metrics (UP-class recall = 0.14).
- EnrichR/external-knowledge injection hurt performance, suggesting prompt-format brittleness.
- CoT factuality is hard to validate at scale; the manual audit is necessarily small.

**Not addressed.**
- **No variance reporting.** Every cell of every table is a single run; "substantially and significantly outperforms" is asserted, never tested. No p-values, no bootstrap CIs.
- **Single benchmark.** All numbers are on PerturbQA; no Replogle 2022 raw splits, no Norman 2019, no Adamson 2016.
- **Single cross-cell fold.** Only RPE1 is held out — no Jurkat-out / K562-out / HepG2-out runs.
- **Single backbone, single teacher.** Only DeepSeek-R1-distilled-Llama-3.1-8B; only o4-mini as teacher. Transferability to Qwen2.5, Llama-3.1-Instruct, GPT-4o-as-teacher, etc. is unknown.
- **Data-leakage risk.** PerturbQA derives from publicly known Perturb-seq screens; o4-mini may have seen these papers in pretraining, so synthetic CoTs may be partly recalling published (perturbation, gene) findings rather than reasoning de novo. No held-out-by-publication-date or held-out-by-gene-family analysis.
- **Approach 2 leakage path.** The CoT-generation prompt contains the ground-truth label. The resulting traces may encode the answer in stylistic patterns the student exploits as a shortcut. No masked-final-token ablation.
- **No data-scaling curve.** The "2% is enough" claim rests on one filtered point vs one unfiltered point.
- **No LoRA-rank or full-FT ablation.**

## Why It Matters for Medical AI

In-silico CRISPRi screening is a near-term bottleneck for target discovery: wet-lab Perturb-seq is expensive, and existing perturbation models (GEARS, scGPT) generalize poorly. SynthPert demonstrates that an 8B LLM trained on a few thousand synthetic mechanistic explanations can outperform much larger LLM-RAG pipelines on the binary DE task — at ~1.5 A100-hours of fine-tuning. That cost profile is compatible with iterative target-screening workflows in industrial drug-discovery settings. The caveats are real: the model is most accurate on well-annotated genes (the authors' own "lantern effect"), the headline metrics are inflated by class imbalance, and we have no evidence yet that this transfers off PerturbQA. As an existence-proof that synthetic reasoning distillation can move the needle on cellular perturbation prediction, the paper is convincing; as a deployment-ready screening tool, it is not.
