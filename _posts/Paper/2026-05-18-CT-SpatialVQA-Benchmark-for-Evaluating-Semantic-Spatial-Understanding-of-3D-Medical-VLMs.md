## TL;DR

- **CT-SpatialVQA** turns the **CT-RATE** test split into a **9,077-QA / 1,601-volume** benchmark that probes six spatial primitives (laterality, longitudinal/vertical, anterior-posterior/depth, medial-lateral, adjacency, extent). QA is generated by GPT-4o, filtered by Gemini 2.5 Flash, and 12% is human-audited (95% LLM-vs-human agreement).
- Eight zero-shot 3D medical VLMs (M3D, MERLIN, RadFM, CT-CHAT, Med3DVLM, VILA-M3, MedGemma-1.5 4B, MedEvalKit) all score **below 50%**: **LLM-as-Jury average 34.08%**, **CT-CHAT best at 43.69%**, **VILA-M3 worst at 28.20%**. A per-category radar shows the **vertical (longitudinal) and AP-depth axes degrade hardest** — i.e., the dimensions that actually require cross-slice integration.
- The benchmark is useful, but the paper's stronger interpretive claims (models "rely on linguistic priors" rather than 3D vision) are **not controlled**: no volume-blinded baseline, no leakage control for CT-CHAT (which is itself trained on CT-RATE), and LLM-as-Jury vs BLEU/ROUGE actively disagree because of answer-length artifacts (CT-Chat / MedEvalKit / MedGemma emit 40-120-word answers against 8-word GT).

## Motivation

Volumetric CT reading is fundamentally a 3D spatial task: radiologists mentally reconstruct anterior/posterior, superior/inferior, and laterality across hundreds of axial slices. Recent "3D" medical VLMs (M3D, MERLIN, RadFM, CT-CHAT, Med3DVLM, VILA-M3, MedGemma, MedEvalKit) are advertised as encoding cross-slice context, but no benchmark isolates whether their answers come from genuine volumetric grounding or from linguistic priors learned on report corpora. CT-SpatialVQA fills that gap by deliberately stripping diagnostic/severity content from the QA, leaving only spatial facts that can in principle be checked against the source report.

![CT-SpatialVQA qualitative failure cases](/assets/images/paper/ct-spatialvqa/fig_p002_01.png)
*Figure 1: Representative CT-SpatialVQA failures — across three volumes, RadFM, MedEvalKit, MedGemma, CT-Chat, Med3DVLM, and MERLIN all return spatially wrong answers (e.g., "lower lobe" vs GT "middle lobe of right lung").*

## Core Innovation

This is a **benchmark-only** contribution — no model, no training, no proposed remedy. The novelty is the construction protocol and the dual-track scoring:

1. **Two-stage LLM QA pipeline.** GPT-4o is prompted to generate 7-10 spatial QA pairs per CT-RATE report from Findings + Impressions only, restricted to six spatial primitives; Gemini 2.5 Flash independently re-reads the report and discards items not supported, hallucinated, ambiguous, or off-topic.
2. **Human audit.** A 12% random subset is reviewed by "trained reviewers with relevant backgrounds" → 95% agreement with the validator.
3. **Zero-shot evaluation.** Each of the eight 3D medical VLMs runs with its own official preprocessing (slice sampling, resize). The report is *not* given to the model — answers must come from the volume.
4. **Dual-track scoring.** LLM-as-Judge with three independent judges (GPT-4o, Gemini 2.5 Flash, Qwen3), aggregated into an LLM-as-Jury headline metric; in parallel, lexical metrics SBERT-cos, BLEU, ROUGE-L, METEOR.

## Method & Architecture

![CT-SpatialVQA pipeline](/assets/images/paper/ct-spatialvqa/fig_p004_01.png)
*Figure 2: CT-SpatialVQA pipeline — GPT-4o generates QA pairs from reports, Gemini 2.5 Flash validates them, and human auditors verify a 12% subset; at evaluation time, each volume is paired with a spatial question and scored via an LLM-as-Jury and traditional text metrics.*

![Example QA pair derived from a CT report](/assets/images/paper/ct-spatialvqa/fig_p005_01.png)
*Figure 3: Example CT volume, source radiology report (Findings + Impressions), and the resulting spatially grounded QA pairs (e.g., "In the anterior neighborhood of the ascending aorta").*

**Source corpus and statistics.** CT-RATE test split → 1,601 unique (volume, report) pairs → **9,077 QA pairs** (avg 5.67 QA/report). Mean question length 10.89 words; mean GT answer 8.45 words. The entire 1,601-volume set is used as evaluation; no train/val split — this is an evaluation resource, not a training corpus. Per-category sample counts are not disclosed.

**Spatial taxonomy.** Six categories: laterality & bilateral symmetry; longitudinal/vertical (sup/inf); anterior-posterior/depth; medial-lateral/centricity; adjacency & containment; extent & boundaries.

**Label provenance.** Answers are *report-extracted*, not radiologist-re-annotated on the volume. This means "ground truth" reflects what the report says, not necessarily what is visible on the volume without that report — a subtle but important biasing factor. Report-style phrases like "left lower lobe" appear verbatim in both Q and A, which can inflate scores of models pretrained on CT-RATE reports.

*Figure 5: Per-category LLM-as-Jury accuracy across six spatial primitives — all eight models drop most sharply on longitudinal (vertical) and anterior-posterior (depth) reasoning, the axes that genuinely demand cross-slice integration. Medial-lateral (centricity) is the easiest, presumably because it can be inferred from a single mid-slice view.*

![Answer length distributions per model](/assets/images/paper/ct-spatialvqa/fig_p006_01.png)
*Figure 4: Answer-length ridge plot — GT answers cluster near 8 words, but CT-Chat, MedEvalKit, and MedGemma frequently emit 40-120-word responses. Long answers can opportunistically contain the right keyword (inflating LLM-as-Judge) while crushing BLEU/ROUGE.*

- **Per-category degradation (Fig. 5).** The sharpest drops are on **longitudinal (vertical) and anterior-posterior (depth)** — the axes that genuinely require cross-slice integration. Medial-lateral / centricity is the easiest category. This is the paper's most interesting empirical signal, even though per-category counts are not disclosed.
- **Answer-length pathology (Fig. 4).** Long-form models (CT-Chat, MedEvalKit, MedGemma) emit 40-120-word answers vs an 8.45-word GT. RadFM has the best BLEU/ROUGE/METEOR (short, report-style outputs maximize lexical overlap) but only middling Jury accuracy. **The two evaluation tracks reward opposite behaviors**, and the paper does not analyze the disagreement.
- **No ablations on the benchmark itself.** No "remove report-leakage words and re-rank", no "give the report back and recover performance", no variance / multiple seeds, single inference run per model.

## Limitations

**Methodological gaps the headline narrative depends on but does not control for:**

1. **CT-CHAT's win is plausibly leakage, not architecture.** CT-CHAT is trained on CT-RATE — the same source corpus the benchmark is built from — yet it tops the table. The paper presents this as a clean ranking and does not flag the contamination.
2. **No volume-blinded / text-only baseline.** Without measuring what an LLM scores when given only the question and no image, the paper cannot separate "model can't see 3D" from "QA is partly answerable from question text alone" (e.g., questions that already name the structure and ask only for laterality). The "models rely on linguistic priors" framing is suggested, not demonstrated.
3. **LLM-as-Jury vs lexical metrics rank models in opposite directions.** RadFM leads BLEU/ROUGE/METEOR; CT-Chat / MedEvalKit / MedGemma lead the Jury. The disagreement is driven by answer length (40-120 words vs 8-word GT). Neither track is unambiguously the "right" one, and the paper picks the LLM-Jury number for the headline without analyzing the artifact.
4. **The 95% human-LLM agreement is on QA-vs-report**, not on whether each answer is visually verifiable against the volume. Reviewer count, clinical credentialing, inter-rater kappa, and per-category audit counts are all undisclosed.
5. **No chance baseline for open-ended QA.** "All models below 50%" is a softer claim than it reads because the answer space is free-form text, not multiple choice.
6. **No held-out non-CT-RATE volumes** to test whether the failure pattern generalizes beyond a single source dataset. No variance, no significance tests, single inference run per model.
7. **Repository inconsistency.** Abstract declares a public GitHub link; Contributions refers to an "anonymous repository" — minor release/anonymity slip.
8. **No proposed remedy.** Purely a problem statement; no method or even an architectural sketch addressing the diagnosed weakness.

## Why It Matters for Medical AI

3D CT VLMs are increasingly pitched as "report-generation-ready" — but report generation that is wrong about laterality, slice level, or depth is worse than no report at all. CT-SpatialVQA is the first benchmark to isolate that failure mode at scale, and the per-category radar (vertical + AP-depth degrade hardest) gives a concrete target for the next round of architectures: cross-slice positional encoding, anatomy-aware projection, or 3D-native tokenization rather than slice-pooled 2D encoders. Two design lessons for follow-up work are already visible in this paper's own gaps — **publish a text-only baseline and a leakage-controlled comparison**, and **either standardize answer length or report length-normalized scores**, otherwise the next benchmark will keep producing rankings that flip depending on which scorer you trust.
